# RD-10A.10: Distinction Preservation Audit

**Status**: COMPLETE  
**Date**: 2026-06-10  
**Purpose**: For each transition, determine whether fertility comes from creating distinctions or from preserving distinctions that already exist in the underlying state space.

---

## Why This Matters

The hidden assumption in the current framework is:

> Every important distinction is created by the topology.

But this may not be true.

A distinction can exist without being protected.  
A topology can protect a distinction without creating it.  
A capability can arise from exploiting distinctions that already existed in the underlying state space.

The question is:

> **Does fertility come from creating distinctions?**  
> **Or does fertility come from preserving distinctions that would otherwise disappear?**

Those are not the same hypothesis.

---

## Method

For each transition:
1. **Distinction Space Available**: What distinctions could exist in the underlying state space?
2. **Distinctions Preserved**: What distinctions actually survive?
3. **Preservation Mechanism**: What topology selects and stabilizes them?
4. **Capability**: What capability emerges from the preserved distinctions?

### Distinctions Preserved

Discrete orbital states (1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, ...). A tiny subset of the available space.

### Preservation Mechanism

Bound state quantization. The Coulomb potential + quantum mechanics selects discrete energy levels and stabilizes them.

### Capability

Chemistry. The periodic table. Chemical reactions.

### Analysis

The distinction between energy levels is not invented by the atom. It already exists in the quantum structure of the system. The atom selects a discrete subset and preserves it.

**Atom ≠ creator of distinction**  
**Atom = selector and protector of distinction**

## The Test

Suppose two universes.

Universe A: Creates many distinctions but cannot preserve them.

Universe B: Creates few distinctions but preserves them indefinitely.

Which becomes fertile?

Almost certainly Universe B.

That suggests preservation may be more fundamental than generation.
