# External Reader Test — Briefing Document

## Strategic Purpose

This is NOT consensus seeking, persuasion, or ontology discussion.
This IS adversarial scientific calibration.

The papers have become internally coherent. That means the remaining failures are invisible from inside the project. The reader test exists to surface:
- hidden assumptions
- terminology drift
- unrecognized overclaiming
- statistical ambiguity
- disciplinary mismatch

**Do NOT defend the framework emotionally.** The project gains credibility by surviving criticism, not by avoiding it.

---

## Materials Sent

Each reader receives ONLY:
1. Paper 1 manuscript (`submission/paper1/manuscript.tex`)
2. Paper 2 manuscript (`submission/paper2/manuscript.tex`)
3. Figures (9 primary: `fig1_collapse_law.pdf` through `fig3_void_search.pdf` + 6 supplementary)
4. Reproducibility repository link (`repository/`)
5. Reproducibility statement (`submission/reproducibility_statement.md`)

NOT included: ontology, sentience framework, E8, cosmology, SGP project documents.

---

## Questions to Ask Every Reader

1. Which claims are unsupported?
2. Which claims are overstated?
3. Which controls are missing?
4. Which statistical assumptions are weak?
5. What would make you reject this?
6. Which result appears strongest?
7. Which result appears least convincing?
8. What additional test is most critical?

**No leading prompts. No defense of the work. No ontology discussion.**

---

## Reader A — Statistical Physicist

**Primary targets**: RG interpretation, criticality framing, scaling arguments, universality terminology

Key areas to probe:
- Is d(N) ~ N^{2.2} really anomalous in the RG sense, or could it fit a known universality class?
- Does the "relevant operator" language (Wilson RG) legitimately apply to subsampling scaling?
- Is the phase diagram language (collapse/persistence regimes) justified?
- Are we overclaiming universality (i.e., claiming the framework applies beyond what the data supports)?
- Does the exponential bound α·PR ≥ 2 have a known parallel in statistical physics?

---

## Reader B — Random Matrix Theorist

**Primary targets**: ensemble distinguishability, finite-size effects, localization statistics, sparse ensemble equivalence

Key areas to probe:
- Is the MEC/GOE distance metric (in σ) properly calibrated? Are the null distributions correct?
- Does the sparse ensemble equivalence at fixed N (0.86σ) actually indicate a problem, or is the scaling separation d(N) ~ N^{2.2} a sufficient resolution?
- Are Wigner-Dyson statistics the right RMT comparison, or should we use other ensembles (chiral, Wishart)?
- Is the IPR/level spacing ratio analysis adequate for eigenvector statistics?
- Could the results be explained by known random matrix models with structured priors?

---

## Reader C — Complex Systems Researcher

**Primary targets**: high-dimensional organization, persistence interpretation, operator comparisons, methodological coherence

Key areas to probe:
- Is "spectral persistence" (broad eigenvalue spectrum with slow decay) a well-defined concept, or does it conflate distinct phenomena?
- Does the operator comparison (correlation vs precision vs VAR) convincingly show that instantaneous correlation geometry is the meaningful operator?
- Are the synthetic data experiments adequate controls, or do they encode the same assumptions they should test?
- Is the methodological framework coherent, or does it mix incompatible tools?

---

## Reader D — Computational Neuroscientist (optional)

**Primary targets**: biological interpretability, preprocessing assumptions, covariance geometry validity

Key areas to probe:
- Are the MEC recordings and preprocessing appropriate for the claims being made?
- Is the biological interpretation (stabilized neural dynamics) justified by the data?
- Could the observed spectral structure arise from known biophysical mechanisms (e.g., balanced E/I, synaptic scaling)?
- Are there recording-specific artifacts that could produce the observed correlation geometry?

## Post-Reading Process

After all responses are collected:
1. Record each criticism in `docs/external_reader_feedback.md`
2. Classify by severity (critical / moderate / minor)
3. Assess validity (valid / partially valid / invalid)
4. Decide on manuscript revision for each
5. If critical issues exist, revise before arXiv
6. Only release to arXiv when all critical issues resolved
