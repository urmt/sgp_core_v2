# T200.2: Dependency Structure of the Six Anthropic Constants

## Overview

This document maps the dependency relationships among the six fundamental constants
under analysis. The purpose is to determine which may legitimately be treated as
independent, which are linked by known physics, and which dependency assumptions
dominate the uncertainty budget of any fine-tuning calculation.

### The Six Constants

| # | Symbol | Name | Dimensionless form |
|---|--------|------|--------------------|
| 1 | α | Fine-structure constant | e²/(4π ε₀ ħ c) |
| 2 | αₛ | Strong coupling constant | g₃²/(4π) |
| 3 | μ | Electron–proton mass ratio | m_e / m_p |
| 4 | α_G | Gravitational coupling | G m_p² / (ħ c) |
| 5 | v/M_Pl | Electroweak scale | v / M_Pl |
| 6 | Λ | Cosmological constant | ρ_Λ / M_Pl⁴ |

### Edge Classification

| Label | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **S** | Strongly dependent — known theoretical link; varying one forces the other |
| **W** | Weakly dependent — known link but sub-dominant or model-dependent |
| **U** | Unknown — no established link in any known theory |
| **X** | Commonly assumed independent but known to be linked under plausible assumptions |

## A. Dependency Matrix

### Summary Matrix

| | α | αₛ | μ | α_G | v/M_Pl | Λ |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **α** | — | **U/X** | **U** | **U** | **U** | **U** |
| **αₛ** | U/X | — | **S** | **S** | **W** | **U** |
| **μ** | U | S | — | **W** | **S†** | **U** |
| **α_G** | U | S | W | — | **U** | **U** |
| **v/M_Pl** | U | W | S† | U | — | **W** |
| **Λ** | U | U | U | U | W | — |

† μ ∝ v only when Higgs Yukawa couplings are held fixed. In the Standard Model,

conditional on the choice of variable parameter set.

### Detailed Per-Cell Analysis

#### Row 1: α (fine-structure constant)

| Pair | Class | Reasoning | Key Citations |
|------|-------|-----------|---------------|
| α ↔ αₛ | **U/X** | In the Standard Model, U(1) and SU(3) gauge couplings are independent. In GUTs (SU(5), SO(10)), they unify at high energy, making α and αₛ at low energy linked via RG running. Most anthropic analyses treat them as independent without addressing this, making this "commonly assumed independent but unjustified" if GUTs are assumed. Hogan (2000) notes only 3 of 5 crucial parameters are independent in the GUT context. | Georgi–Quinn–Weinberg (1974); Langacker (1981); Hogan (2000); Uzan (2003, 2011) |
| α ↔ μ | **U** | No known theoretical link in the Standard Model or beyond. μ depends on the electron Yukawa coupling and QCD scale; α is an independent gauge coupling. QED corrections to m_p are ~0.1% of m_p, making any dependence negligible at anthropic precision. | — |
| α ↔ α_G | **U** | No known link between electromagnetism and gravity at the fundamental level. Some quantum-gravity and extra-dimension models could connect them, but no established relation. | — |
| α ↔ v/M_Pl | **U** | The Higgs vev and the fine-structure constant are independent parameters in the SM. The weak mixing angle sin²θ_W relates α to g₂, not to v. No known direct link. | — |
| α ↔ Λ | **U** | No known connection between QED coupling and vacuum energy density. Inconsistent with any established theory. | — |

#### Row 2: αₛ (strong coupling constant)

| Pair | Class | Reasoning | Key Citations |
|------|-------|-----------|---------------|
| αₛ ↔ μ | **S** | **Strong dependence.** The proton mass m_p is dominated by the QCD scale Λ_QCD, which is generated by dimensional transmutation: Λ_QCD ∝ M_Pl exp(−2π/(β₀ αₛ)). The electron mass m_e comes from the Higgs Yukawa. Therefore μ = m_e/m_p depends exponentially on αₛ through m_p. A 1% change in αₛ changes Λ_QCD by ~exp(1/(αₛ²)) — nonlinear, potentially orders of magnitude. This is one of the strongest dependencies in the set. | Yang et al. (2018) lattice QCD proton mass decomposition; Carr & Rees (1979) |
| αₛ ↔ α_G | **S** | **Strong dependence.** α_G = G m_p²/(ħc) depends on m_p². Since m_p is set by Λ_QCD which is exponentially sensitive to αₛ, α_G inherits this sensitivity. This is the origin of the celebrated "large number coincidence" (Dirac 1937): α_G ~ 5×10⁻³⁹ is small because αₛ makes Λ_QCD ≪ M_Pl. | Dicke (1961); Carr & Rees (1979); Dirac (1937) |
| αₛ ↔ v/M_Pl | **W** | Weak dependence via threshold effects. Agrawal et al. (1998) note Λ_QCD ∝ v^β with β ~ 0.25–0.3 when the Higgs vev varies and Yukawas are held fixed. This is a subdominant effect: changing v by ×10 changes Λ_QCD by ~×2, while changing αₛ by 1% changes Λ_QCD exponentially. | Agrawal et al. (1998); Hogan (2000) |
| αₛ ↔ Λ | **U** | No known direct link between the strong coupling constant and the cosmological constant. | — |

#### Row 3: μ = m_e/m_p

| Pair | Class | Reasoning | Key Citations |
|------|-------|-----------|---------------|
| μ ↔ α_G | **W** | Both μ and α_G depend on m_p. α_G ∝ m_p² and μ ∝ 1/m_p. If m_p is the only parameter changing (via αₛ variation), then α_G ∝ 1/μ² — an anti-correlation. However, μ also depends on m_e, which is independent of α_G, making the link indirect. | — |
| μ ↔ v/M_Pl | **S†** | **Strong dependence, conditional.** m_e = y_e · v/√2, so if y_e (electron Yukawa) is held fixed, m_e ∝ v directly. The proton mass m_p has a small quark-mass component (~9% of total) that also varies with v, but the dominant effect is m_e ∝ v. Physically: if v doubles, μ doubles when all other dimensionless parameters are fixed. | Yang et al. (2018); Agrawal et al. (1998) |
| μ ↔ Λ | **U** | No known direct link. μ describes particle mass ratios; Λ describes vacuum energy. | — |

#### Row 4: α_G (gravitational coupling)

| Pair | Class | Reasoning | Key Citations |
|------|-------|-----------|---------------|
| α_G ↔ v/M_Pl | **U** | No known direct theoretical link. G and the Higgs vev are independent parameters. In extra-dimension scenarios (ADD, RS), the effective G could be related to v via the size of extra dimensions, but no established relation. | — |
| α_G ↔ Λ | **U** | Both involve gravity, but at vastly different scales and for different reasons. Some attempts at quantum gravity connect them (e.g., holographic dark energy), but no standard relation. | — |

#### Row 5: v/M_Pl (electroweak scale)

| Pair | Class | Reasoning | Key Citations |
|------|-------|-----------|---------------|
| v/M_Pl ↔ Λ | **W** | The Standard Model Higgs potential contributes ~λ v⁴ ~ 10⁸ GeV⁴ to the vacuum energy. For the observed Λ to be ~10⁻⁴⁷ GeV⁴, this requires a cancellation of ~55 orders of magnitude. If v changes, the predicted (but not observed) Higgs contribution to Λ changes as v⁴. However, since we do not understand the cancellation mechanism, whether v-variation leads to Λ-variation depends on the unknown cancellation mechanism. This is the cosmological constant problem. | Weinberg (1989); Martin (2012) |

## B. Dependency Graph

### Directed Graph Representation

```
    α ──U── αₛ ──S── μ ──S── v/M_Pl ──W── Λ
                 │                    │
                 ├──S── α_G           │
                 │                    │
                 └──W── v/M_Pl        │
                                      │
α_G ──U── v/M_Pl ──────────────────────┘
α_G ──U── Λ
α   ──U── all others
```

### Key Structural Observations

1. **αₛ is the hub.** It has the most connections (S/W with 3 of 5 other constants). Any fine-tuning analysis that varies αₛ must simultaneously account for μ and α_G and should account for v/M_Pl.

2. **α is the isolate.** It has no strong or weak dependencies on any other constant in the set, under any known theory. This means α can be treated as independent from the rest — a rare genuinely free parameter.

3. **Λ is nearly isolated.** Its only link is a weak, poorly understood connection to v/M_Pl via the Higgs vacuum energy.

4. **The μ–αₛ–α_G triangle** forms a strongly coupled subsystem. Changing any one of these changes the others. Multiplying probabilities across this triangle is formally invalid unless the joint probability distribution is known.

5. **The μ–v/M_Pl link** is the most commonly ignored dependency. Many analyses vary v or μ independently without noting that m_e ∝ v when Yukawas are fixed.

### Subsystem Decomposition

The 6 constants decompose naturally into dependency subsystems:

- **Subsystem A (isolated):** α — no known dependencies
- **Subsystem B (strongly coupled):** αₛ, μ, α_G — all inter-dependent
- **Subsystem C (weakly coupled):** v/M_Pl — weakly linked to αₛ and Λ
- **Subsystem D (nearly isolated):** Λ — weakly linked to v/M_Pl only

## C. Assumption Audit of T200.1 Sources

For each major source from the T200.1 literature survey, this section documents:
(1) what dependency assumptions were made, (2) whether they were justified, and (3) whether they were ignored.

| Assumption | Justified? | Notes |

### Summary Pattern

Of the major sources surveyed:

- **Most (7/9)** vary one parameter at a time without addressing whether the others should be allowed to co-vary.
- **Only 2 (Uzan, Tegmark)** explicitly warn that this is a problem.
- **Only 1 (Agrawal)** provides a principled justification for which parameters vary.
- **All** single-parameter bounds should be interpreted as upper bounds on sensitivity, not as the actual probability mass for that parameter.

## D. Risk Assessment

### Per-Constant Ranking

Rank each constant by two axes: (1) confidence in the anthropic bound, (2) confidence in the independence assumption.

| Constant | Confidence in anthropic bound | Confidence in independence | Overall risk |
|----------|------------------------------|---------------------------|-------------|
| **α** | **High** | **High** (isolated) | **Low** |
| **αₛ** | **Low** (contested 2% vs. 50%) | **Low** (hub of dependencies) | **Very High** |
| **μ** | **None** (no bound exists) | **Low** (depends on αₛ, v) | **Very High** |
| **α_G** | **Medium** (through stellar lifetimes) | **Low** (depends on αₛ via m_p) | **High** |
| **v/M_Pl** | **Low** (contested: Agrawal vs. Harnik) | **Medium** (weakly coupled to αₛ, Λ) | **High** |
| **Λ** | **High** | **Medium** (weakly coupled to v) | **Medium** |

### Ranking by Risk Level

| Risk | Constants | Reason |
|------|-----------|--------|
| **Very High** | αₛ, μ | αₛ has contested anthropic bounds AND is the dependency hub. μ has no anthropic bound AND is dependent on both αₛ and v. |
| **High** | α_G, v/M_Pl | α_G has medium confidence bound but strong dependence on αₛ. v/M_Pl has contested bound and weak dependence on others. |
| **Medium** | Λ | Well-established bound, but weakly coupled to v via Higgs vacuum energy problem. |
| **Low** | α | Well-understood bounds, genuinely isolated from all other constants. |

### Dominant Risk: The αₛ–μ–α_G Triangle

The most important finding of this dependency analysis is that **three of the six constants form a strongly coupled subsystem**:

- αₛ determines Λ_QCD, which determines m_p (∼95% of m_p)
- m_p inversely determines μ = m_e/m_p
- m_p² determines α_G = G m_p²/(ħc)

Any calculation that assigns independent probabilities to αₛ, μ, and α_G and then multiplies them is committing a formal error. The actual degrees of freedom in this subsystem are:

- **1 true degree of freedom** (αₛ), from which both μ and α_G derive (if all other SM parameters are fixed)
- **2 degrees of freedom** (αₛ + y_e, the electron Yukawa), if m_e is also allowed to vary

The common practice of quoting separate fine-tuning numbers for each of these three constants and aggregating them is unsupported by known physics.

### Sensitivity of the Final Budget

The dependency assumptions that dominate the final uncertainty budget, ranked:

1. **αₛ–μ–α_G coupling** (overall #1): Determines whether the subsystem represents 1 or 3 independent degrees of freedom
2. **GUT assumption**: Determines whether α and αₛ are linked (adding another coupling)
3. **μ–v link**: Determines whether varying v changes μ (conditional on Yukawa treatment)
4. **v–Λ link**: Determines whether changing v changes the cosmological constant problem
5. **α isolation**: Consistently low risk — α is genuinely independent

## E. Answers to Specific Questions

### Q1: Can α and αₛ be varied independently in the literature, or do GUT models link them?

**Answer:** In the Standard Model, yes — they are independent gauge couplings. In GUTs (SU(5), SO(10)), the RG running forces them to a common value at high energy, linking them. The literature is split: Carr & Rees, Barrow & Tipler, and others treat them independently; Hogan (2000) and Uzan (2003, 2011) note the GUT link and question the independence. Most anthropic bounds for α assume αₛ is held fixed, and vice versa, without justification.

### Q2: Is μ independent, or largely inherited from deeper parameters?

**Answer:** μ is **largely inherited.** m_p is determined by αₛ via Λ_QCD. m_e is determined by the Higgs vev v and the electron Yukawa y_e. So μ = m_e/m_p inherits dependence on:
- αₛ (through m_p) — **strong dependence**
- v (through m_e) — **strong dependence, conditional on y_e fixed**
- y_e (directly) — independent

Under any plausible variation scheme, μ is at most a derived quantity, not a fundamental degree of freedom.

### Q3: Are α_G and Λ genuinely separable in anthropic analyses?

**Answer:** Yes, they are separable in current analyses. No known theory links G (Newton's constant) to Λ (vacuum energy) in a way that would couple their anthropic bounds. Both are "unknown" dependency pairs.

### Q4: Which published fine-tuning calculations multiply probabilities for parameters that are not known to be independent?

**Answer:** Specifically problematic are calculations that:
- Multiply bounds for αₛ, μ, and α_G (the triangle)
- Multiply bounds for v/M_Pl and μ without adjusting for their proportionality
- Multiply bounds for αₛ and α without addressing GUT assumptions

The most comprehensive offender is Barrow & Tipler (1986), which catalogs multiple coincidences and presents them as a cumulative case without addressing dependencies. Some later review articles (e.g., Lewis & Barnes 2016) present multiplication tables without dependency correction.

### Q5: Which dependency assumptions dominate the final uncertainty budget?

**Answer:** See Section D above. The αₛ–μ–α_G coupling is the single largest source of uncertainty in any cumulative fine-tuning calculation. Whether this subsystem counts as 1 or 3 independent parameters changes the "number of tuned parameters" from 6 to as few as 4, and the joint probability by many orders of magnitude.

## F. Implications for T200.3 and T200.4

1. **T200.3 (Probability Frameworks)** cannot assign independent probabilities to αₛ, μ, and α_G. They must be treated as a joint distribution or the analysis must reduce to the subspace of truly independent parameters.

2. **T200.4 (Sensitivity Analysis)** should prioritize:
   - Varying the subsystem dimension (1 vs. 2 vs. 3 independent parameters in the αₛ–μ–α_G group)
   - Testing GUT-dependence (α–αₛ coupled vs. uncoupled)
   - Testing the μ–v conditional link (Yukawas fixed vs. free)
   - Comparing Agrawal vs. Harnik bounds for v/M_Pl

3. **A critical meta-point:** The strongest result of T200.2 is not a dependency graph but a negative result — that some probability products commonly quoted in the fine-tuning literature cannot be justified because the dependency structure is too poorly understood. This should be a central finding of the T200 output.
