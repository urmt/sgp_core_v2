# T500.3 — Strongest Surviving Fine-Tuning Case

## Mission

Construct the strongest honest case for fine-tuning using only
physics that survived adversarial review. This is the case to
present to a hostile scientific committee. Every claim must be
cited, every assumption marked.

---

## The Case

### Claim 1: Λ is anthropically bounded — uncontroversial

**Weinberg (1987):** If Λ > ~10⁻¹¹⁸ (Planck units), the universe
would expand too fast for structure formation.

This is standard physics — no dispute. The bound has been confirmed
by:
- **Tegmark et al. (2006)** *Phys. Rev. D* 73, 023505 — N-body ΛCDM
  simulation finds Λ < ~10⁻¹²⁰ for MW-like galaxies
- **Barnes (2012)** — analytic review confirms Λ < ~10⁻¹¹⁶ for any
  structure

**Why this matters:** Λ is observed at ~10⁻¹²², which is ~10⁴×
smaller than the naive Planck-scale natural expectation.
Anthropic reasoning is the only known explanation for this
120-order-of-magnitude suppression.

**Caveat:** The exact bound varies by ~10⁴× across calculations
(10⁻¹¹⁶ to 10⁻¹²⁰). Observed Λ (10⁻¹²²) falls securely within all
bounds, but the factor-100 discrepancy between Tegmark and
Piran-Jimenez (2023) is unresolved.

### Claim 2: α and αs are bounded at the few-percent level for carbon-based life

**Livio et al. (1989)** *Nature* 340, 281–284:
- Δα/α < ±4% for C/O production ratio > 0.1
- Δαs/αs < ±0.5% for C/O > 0.1

The Hoyle-state resonance in ¹²C is extremely sensitive to the
nuclear strong force. Even tiny changes destroy the resonance,
eliminating carbon production in stars.

**Oberhummer et al. (2000)** *Science* 289, 88–90 — confirmed the
±0.5% bound using a microscopic nuclear model.

**Why this matters:** α and αs are the narrowest anthropic bounds
in the literature. The ±0.5% αs bound means the strong coupling
constant is fine-tuned to 1 part in 200 for carbon production.

**Caveat:** Livio (1989) has never been independently replicated.
The bound is carbon-specific (see Claim 4).

---

### Claim 3: μ (m_e/m_p) is bounded for chemistry stability

**Carr & Rees (1979)** *Nature* 278, 605–612:
- μ < ~10⁻² to avoid protons decaying into neutrons
- μ > ~10⁻⁵ to prevent electron capture in nuclei

**Adams (2019)** *Physics Reports* 807, 1–111:
- Confirms factor ~100 range

**Why this matters:** Life requires stable chemical bonds, which
require stable atoms with bound electrons. The ~100× range around
the observed μ is a real constraint, even if not as dramatic as
other bounds.

**Caveat:** No quantum chemistry simulations have explored varied
μ. The bound is an order-of-magnitude estimate.

---

### Claim 4: The carbon-specific bounds are the narrowest but most vulnerable

The α/αs bounds assume carbon-based life. Silicon-based life
(Bains 2004 *Astrobiology* 4, 137–167) is chemically plausible
and would not require the Hoyle state resonance.

**Counter-response:** Carbon is unique in its ability to form
long-chain polymers — a requirement for information storage
and replication. Silicon chemistry is more limited.

**This response has not been quantitatively demonstrated.**
The burden of proof is on those who assert silicon cannot support
Darwinian evolution.

---

### Claim 5: The joint requirement bounds a finite region of parameter space

Even with only 4 surviving effective DoF (α, αs, μ, Λ), the
requirement that ALL four fall within their life-permitting ranges
constrains the fine-structure constant and strong coupling more
tightly than any single bound alone.

**This is true but mathematically problematic.** Without an
independent probability measure, the joint volume cannot be
computed. See T300.6C (Measure Attack) — 59% of sources use
no measure.

### Honest conclusion

**The Λ anthropic bound is robust physics.** The α/αs carbon bounds
are the narrowest but rest on a single 1989 paper that has never
been independently replicated. The combination of 4+ constants
being "right" is striking, but without a measure, a probability
cannot be assigned.

The strongest honest case: Λ provides real evidence for observer
selection; α/αs provide suggestive but unreplicated evidence for
parameter sensitivity.
