# T200.1 — Constants & Literature Survey

**Program:** T200 — Independent Fine-Tuning Analysis
**Phase:** 1 (literature survey — collect all published bounds per constant)
**Document purpose:** Answer the question: *"What ranges have been claimed, by whom, under what assumptions, and with what level of disagreement?"* — without computing probabilities.
**Cross-reference:** T200.0 defines all terms used herein (tiers, measures, operational rules).

---

## How to Read This Document

Each constant section has a uniform structure:
1. **Identity** — symbol, name, measured value, dimensionless form
2. **Why It Matters** — brief physical role, which tiers it affects
3. **Life-Permitting Criterion** — what must remain possible for the universe to be viable
4. **Claimed Bounds** — table of published ranges with full metadata per entry
5. **Metadata per bound:** what varied, what held fixed, one-at-a-time or multi-parameter, definition of life-permitting, computational method, independence assumptions
6. **Strongest Fine-Tuning Argument** — the narrowest claimed bound, with strongest support
7. **Strongest Critique/Alternative** — paper(s) arguing for wider bounds or different interpretation
8. **Confidence assessment** — Firm / Moderate / Weak per bound

Bounds from different papers are **never merged**. All distinct bounds are preserved with their citations.

## Section 1 — α (Fine-Structure Constant)

### Identity

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Symbol | α = e²/(4π ε₀ ħ c) |
| Name | Fine-structure constant / Sommerfeld constant |
| Measured value | 1/137.035999084(21) = 0.0072973525693(11) (CODATA 2018) |
| Dimensionless? | Yes — pure number |
| Physical role | Strength of electromagnetic interaction; determines atomic energy levels, molecular bond energies, scattering cross-sections |
| Affected tiers | 1 (stable chemistry), 2 (stellar fusion rates), 3 (complex chemistry / carbon production), 4 (information processing) |

### Life-Permitting Criterion

- **Tier 1 (stable chemistry):** α must allow at least one stable bound state for electron-proton (hydrogen atom). If α is too large, the electron's ground-state binding energy exceeds pair-production threshold (2 m_e c²), causing spontaneous pair creation and atomic instability. If α is too small, binding is too weak for stable atoms.
- **Tier 2 (long-lived stars):** α controls radiative opacity in stellar interiors via bound-free and free-free transitions; also determines pp-chain reaction rates (Coulomb barrier penetration).
- **Tier 3 (complex chemistry):** Carbon production via triple-alpha process is sensitive to the Hoyle-state resonance energy, which depends on α and αs jointly. Oxygen production also sensitive.
- **Tier 4 (information processing):** Molecular bond energies and solvent properties (e.g., water's dipole moment) scale with α.

### Claimed Bounds

| Bound | Range | Tier | Source | Method | Confidence |
|-------|-------|------|--------|--------|------------|
| Upper (chemistry fails) | α > 0.1 — atoms unstable (pair production) | 1 | Barrow & Tipler (1986) §4.2 | Analytic: ground-state binding > 2m_e c² | Firm |
| Lower (chemistry fails) | α < 0.001 — binding too weak for stable molecules | 1 | Barrow & Tipler (1986) §4.2 | Analytic: Bohr radius exceeds galactic scales | Moderate |
| Upper (stellar fusion) | α/α₀ < ~1.1 — pp-chain rate too slow or too fast | 2 | Adams (2019) §3.1 | Stellar structure models | Moderate |
| Upper (carbon production) | Δα/α < 4% relative to observed | 3 | Livio et al. (1989) Nature 340, 281 | Triple-alpha reaction network, Hoyle state energies varied | Moderate |
| Lower (carbon production) | Δα/α < 4% relative to observed (symmetric or asymmetric?) | 3 | Livio et al. (1989) | Same computation | Moderate |
| Combined (α + αs, triple-alpha) | Δα/α < 4% AND Δαs/αs < 0.5% for carbon | 3 | Livio et al. (1989) | Joint variation of Hoyle state energy | Weak (2-parameter) |
| Varying α (astrophysical constraints) | Δα/α < 10⁻⁶ over cosmic time | N/A (not life-permitting) | Bronnikov et al. (2025) arXiv:2506.18328 | Quasar absorption spectra | N/A (observational, not anthropic) |

**Notes on merging:** The Barrow & Tipler bounds (Tier 1) span ~2 orders of magnitude. The Livio et al. bounds (Tier 3) are ~4% fractional. These are not contradictory — they address different tiers. The Adams (2019) review compiles multiple independent bounds and notes that α must be within roughly an order of magnitude of its observed value for stellar fusion, but the exact range depends on which stellar properties are required.

### Metadata per Bound (Livio et al. 1989 — the most cited quantitative bound)

- **What varied:** α and nuclear resonance energies (coupled through underlying QED corrections)
- **What held fixed:** αs (strong coupling), particle masses, G, weak scale, Λ
- **One-at-a-time or multi-parameter:** Primarily one-at-a-time for α; joint variation with strong force noted but not fully explored
- **Definition of life-permitting:** "Appreciable carbon production" — defined as C/O ratio > 0.1 after helium burning
- **Computational model:** Stellar nucleosynthesis network for triple-alpha process, with Hoyle-state energy treated as free parameter
- **Independence assumptions:** α and αs treated as independent (likely not true — both are coupling constants of a potentially unified theory)

### Strongest Fine-Tuning Argument

Livio et al. (1989): The Hoyle state in ¹²C lies at 7.656 MeV above ground. If this energy were shifted by more than ~4% through changes in α (with αs fixed), carbon production drops by >90%. This is a **moderate-confidence** bound because the nuclear physics is well-understood but the mapping from α to Hoyle-state energy is model-dependent.

Barrow & Tipler (1986): α must be within roughly two orders of magnitude of its observed value for any chemistry at all. **Firm** for the upper bound (pair production instability at α > 0.1), **moderate** for the lower bound.

### Strongest Critique/Alternative

Hogan (2000) Rev. Mod. Phys. 72, 1149: Notes that many "fine-tuning" claims for α conflate different tiers of life-permitting criteria. The strictest bound (Livio et al. 4%) applies only to carbon-based life, not all possible forms of organic chemistry.

MacDonald & Mullan (2009) PRD 80, 043507: Show that the "2% stronger strong force => diproton => hydrogen catastrophe" argument (sometimes applied to α via grand unification) is overstated; substantial hydrogen survives for up to 50% variation in the strong coupling.

Stenger (2011) *The Fallacy of Fine-Tuning*: Argues that α can vary by orders of magnitude and still allow some form of stable chemistry, depending on compensatory changes in other constants.

## Section 2 — αs (Strong Coupling Constant)

### Identity

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Symbol | αs(M_Z) |
| Name | Strong coupling constant (at Z-boson mass scale) |
| Measured value | 0.1179 ± 0.0009 (PDG 2022) |
| Dimensionless? | Yes, but scale-dependent (running coupling) |
| Physical role | Strength of the strong nuclear force; determines QCD scale Λ_QCD (~200 MeV), which sets nucleon masses and nuclear binding |
| Affected tiers | 1 (stable nuclei), 2 (stellar fusion via pp-chain and CNO), 3 (carbon/oxygen production), 4 (biochemistry foundations) |

### Life-Permitting Criterion

- **Tier 1 (stable chemistry):** αs must allow at least one bound nucleon-nucleon state (deuteron or similar) for the first step of nucleosynthesis. If too weak, no stable nuclei beyond protons. If too strong, diprotons are stable, all H converts to He in Big Bang (but see critique below).
- **Tier 2 (long-lived stars):** αs determines nuclear reaction rates (Coulomb barriers are fixed by α, but Q-values depend on strong binding). The pp-chain requires deuteron to be bound.
- **Tier 3 (complex chemistry):** The triple-alpha (3He → ¹²C) resonance energy is exquisitely sensitive to strong force binding energies. The 0⁺ Hoyle state at 7.656 MeV must be within ~0.06 MeV of the combined energy of three α particles for sufficient carbon production.
- **Tier 4 (information processing):** Nucleic acids and proteins depend on carbon chemistry enabled by Tier 3.

### Claimed Bounds

| Bound | Range | Tier | Source | Method | Confidence |
|-------|-------|------|--------|--------|------------|
| Upper (diproton bound) | Δαs/αs > ~0.5% → ⁴He below ³He in mass | 1 | Adams (2019) §3.2.1 | Nuclear binding energy calculations | Firm (but significance disputed) |
| Upper (diproton scenario) | Δαs/αs < ~50% for hydrogen survival | 1 | MacDonald & Mullan (2009) PRD 80, 043507 | BBN network with variable αs | Moderate |
| Triple-alpha (carbon) | Δαs/αs < 0.5% for C/O > 0.1 | 3 | Livio et al. (1989); also Oberhummer et al. (2000) | Triple-alpha rate with varied nuclear binding | Moderate |
| Triple-alpha (oxygen) | Δαs/αs < 1% for O not to consume all C | 3 | Livio et al. (1989) | Same network | Moderate |
| Lower (no deuteron) | αs < ~0.06 → no bound deuteron | 1 | Barrow & Tipler (1986) §5.1 | Analytic: nucleon-nucleon potential | Firm |

**Critical meta-observation:** The "diproton catastrophe" claim (αs +2% => diprotons stable => all H fused in Big Bang => no water, no life) is widely repeated but overstated. MacDonald & Mullan (2009) provide explicit BBN calculations showing that substantial hydrogen remains for increases up to ~50%. The 0.5% bound is real for carbon production (Tier 3), which is narrower than any Tier 1 bound.

### Metadata per Bound (Livio et al. 1989 / Oberhummer et al. 2000)

- **What varied:** αs (parametrized as change in nuclear binding energies)
- **What held fixed:** α, G, me/mp, weak scale, Λ
- **One-at-a-time or multi-parameter:** One-at-a-time for carbon bound; joint (α, αs) variation noted
- **Definition of life-permitting:** C/O > 0.1 (enough C for complex chemistry without complete O depletion)
- **Computational model:** Stellar nucleosynthesis reaction network for helium burning
- **Independence assumptions:** α and αs assumed independent; the Hoyle-state energy depends on both, but their joint variation space was sampled only coarsely

### Strongest Fine-Tuning Argument

**Livio et al. (1989):** The ¹²C Hoyle state must lie at 7.596–7.716 MeV (vs. observed 7.656 MeV) for C/O > 0.1. This corresponds to Δαs/αs < 0.5% if αs alone determines the binding shift. This is the most stringent anthropic bound in the literature for any dimensionless constant.

**Widely repeated claim (strong force 2%):** The claim that a 2% increase makes diprotons stable and destroys all hydrogen (Davies 1993, *The Accidental Universe*) is the most commonly cited "fine-tuning" of the strong force. However, it is **not the strongest bound** — the carbon bound (0.5%) is stricter, and the diproton claim is disputed.

### Strongest Critique/Alternative

**MacDonald & Mullan (2009):** Show that the "2% diproton catastrophe" is incorrect. Using BBN network calculations with varied αs, they find hydrogen survives for increases up to 50%. The universe would be different but still potentially habitable — especially if non-carbon life forms are considered.

**Adams (2019) §3.2.2:** Reviews the deuterium instability case (what if αs were *weaker* and deuterium were unbound?). Finds that stars can still function through gravitational contraction and alternative fusion pathways (triple-nucleon reaction). "Universes with no stable deuterium are thus potentially habitable."

**Grohs & Adams (2019):** Further show that even without any stable isotopes beyond hydrogen, gravitational contraction in stars can provide sufficient energy for habitable planets, challenging the necessity of the strong force being anywhere near its observed value.

## Section 3 — μ = m_e/m_p (Electron-to-Proton Mass Ratio)

### Identity

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Symbol | μ = m_e/m_p |
| Name | Electron-to-proton mass ratio |
| Measured value | 1/1836.15267343(11) ≈ 5.446 × 10⁻⁴ |
| Dimensionless? | Yes |
| Physical role | Determines atomic length scales (Bohr radius relative to Compton wavelength), molecular vibration frequencies, stellar opacity via free-free absorption, and the boundary between classical and quantum behavior in solids |
| Affected tiers | 1 (stable chemistry — molecular binding), 2 (stellar structure — opacity), 3 (complex molecules — bond dynamics), 4 (biochemical kinetics) |

### Life-Permitting Criterion

- **Tier 1 (stable chemistry):** If μ is too large (electron too massive), molecular bonds are too rigid; chemical reactions effectively stop. If μ is too small (electron too light), zero-point motion disrupts molecular structure.
- **Tier 2 (long-lived stars):** μ determines the electron scattering opacity in stellar envelopes; also affects the pp-chain rate via the electron screening factor. If μ is too different from observed, stellar structure changes dramatically.
- **Tier 3 (complex chemistry):** Molecular bond lengths scale as a₀ = ħ/(α m_e c). Bond vibration frequencies scale as ω_vib ∝ μ^(1/2). If μ changes significantly, the entire landscape of molecular chemistry shifts.

### Claimed Bounds

| Bound | Range | Tier | Source | Method | Confidence |
|-------|-------|------|--------|--------|------------|
| Upper (chemical dynamics) | μ < ~0.01 — enough for chemistry (qualitative) | 1 | Carr & Rees (1979) | Electron screening and molecular binding estimates | Weak |
| Combined (stellar opacity) | μ must be within ~factor 10 of observed | 2 | Adams (2019) §4.2 | Stellar structure models | Weak |
| Complex molecules | No quantitative bound in literature | 3 | — | — | UNKNOWN |
| Upper (black hole formation) | μ < 0.42 — Chandrasekhar limit constraint | 1 | Barrow & Tipler (1986) | Degenerate electron pressure | Firm but very wide |

**Assessment:** The electron/proton mass ratio has received **far less quantitative anthropic analysis** than α, αs, α_G, or Λ. Most treatments are qualitative — noting that chemistry would be different if μ changed significantly, but not specifying a precise life-permitting range. This is a genuine gap in the literature.

### Metadata per Bound (Carr & Rees 1979)

- **What varied:** μ (implicitly — no explicit parametrization)
- **What held fixed:** All other constants
- **One-at-a-time:** Yes
- **Definition of life-permitting:** "Chemistry as we know it possible"
- **Computational model:** Qualitative physical reasoning, not quantitative simulation
- **Independence assumptions:** μ treated as independent; in QCD, m_p is set by Λ_QCD, while m_e is set by Higgs Yukawa coupling — unrelated in Standard Model

### Strongest Fine-Tuning Argument

There is **no strong published quantitative bound** for μ alone. The strongest claims come from the *combination* of μ with α (since the Bohr radius a₀ = ħ/(α m_e c) depends on both). When α and μ are varied together, the space of viable molecular chemistry may be constrained, but the separate contribution of μ is poorly isolated.

### Strongest Critique/Alternative

Hogan (2000) notes that μ is not independently anthropically constrained — it is the dimensionless *combination* (α, μ, αs, etc.) that matters for chemistry. Stenger (2011) argues that μ can vary by several orders of magnitude while still allowing stable atoms and molecules, as long as α varies compensatorily.

## Section 4 — α_G = G m_p²/(ħc) (Gravitational Coupling Constant)

### Identity

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Symbol | α_G |
| Name | Gravitational coupling constant |
| Formula | α_G = G m_p² / (ħ c) |
| Measured value | ≈ 5.9 × 10⁻³⁹ |
| Dimensionless? | Yes |
| Physical role | Strength of gravity relative to other forces; determines the mass scale for stars (Jeans mass, Chandrasekhar mass), galaxy formation threshold, cosmological structure growth |
| Affected tiers | 2 (star formation), 3 (heavy element production via stellar evolution), 4 (planetary environments) |

### Life-Permitting Criterion

- **Tier 2 (long-lived stars):** α_G determines the minimum mass for stable hydrogen burning (~0.08 M_⊙) and the main-sequence lifetime. If α_G were much larger, stars would be smaller, burn faster, and have shorter lifetimes. If much smaller, stars might not form at all (Jeans mass too large).
- **Tier 3 (complex chemistry):** Requires supernovae to disperse heavy elements. The iron core collapse mechanism depends on the Chandrasekhar mass M_Ch ∝ α_G^(−3/2). If α_G is too large, M_Ch is too small for massive stars to form; if too small, all matter would be in black holes.
- **Tier 4 (information processing):** Requires stable planetary orbits on Gyr timescales, which depend on the balance between gravitational binding and stellar radiation.

### Claimed Bounds

| Bound | Range | Tier | Source | Method | Confidence |
|-------|-------|------|--------|--------|------------|
| Galaxy formation | α_G must be ~within factor 10³–10⁴ of observed | 2 | Carr & Rees (1979) Nature 278, 605 | Jeans mass, cooling time, molecular hydrogen formation | Moderate |
| Star formation | α_G ≲ 10⁻³⁰ (upper) for stars not to be too small | 2 | Carr & Rees (1979) | Minimum stellar mass > 0.01 M_⊙ for fusion | Moderate |
| Star formation | α_G ≳ 10⁻⁴⁵ (lower) for stars to form at all | 2 | Carr & Rees (1979) | Maximum Jeans mass for gravitational collapse | Weak |
| Stellar lifetime | α_G/α_G0 within ~factor 10 for lifetime > 1 Gyr | 2 | Adams (2019) §5.1 | Main-sequence stellar evolution | Moderate |
| Supernova nucleosynthesis | α_G within ~factor 100 of observed for Fe production | 3 | Adams (2019) §5.2 | Core collapse models with varied gravity | Weak |

**Critical observation:** The claimed range (α_G must lie within "several orders of magnitude" of observed) is unusually wide compared to other constants. Carr & Rees (1979) are explicit about this: the anthropic constraints on α_G are among the weakest in the analysis. This contradicts the intuition that "if gravity were slightly stronger, the universe would collapse" — the collapse bound is actually very permissive, and the real constraints come from star formation thresholds.

### Metadata per Bound (Carr & Rees 1979 — most cited)

- **What varied:** G (via α_G), with m_p held fixed
- **What held fixed:** All other fundamental constants
- **One-at-a-time or multi-parameter:** One-at-a-time
- **Definition of life-permitting:** Galaxies exist AND stable stars exist
- **Computational model:** Analytic scaling relations (Jeans mass, cooling time, Chandrasekhar mass, nuclear burning timescale)
- **Independence assumptions:** α_G treated as independent; if G varies with m_p held fixed, the physics of star formation changes, but the interrelation of G with other constants (e.g., in a theory of quantum gravity) is unknown

### Strongest Fine-Tuning Argument

Carr & Rees (1979): For the universe to contain **both** galaxies and stable stars, α_G must be within a few orders of magnitude of 5.9 × 10⁻³⁹. This is a **moderate-confidence** bound — the physics is well-understood but the threshold for "galaxy formation" versus "star formation" admits subjective choices.

Adams (2019) §5: Updates Carr & Rees with modern stellar models, finding that main-sequence stars with lifetimes > 1 Gyr exist for α_G within about a factor of 10 of the observed value. This is narrower than Carr & Rees but still much wider than typical α or αs bounds.

### Strongest Critique/Alternative

Adams (2019) §5.3: Notes that even if α_G were much larger, the universe could still be habitable via **alternative energy sources** — gravitational contraction can power stars for extended periods. The conventional fine-tuning argument assumes that only nuclear fusion can power stars, which is false.

Harnik, Kribs, Perez (2006): In a universe without weak interactions, the role of gravity in forming structures changes. Some structures attributed to weak-scale physics might be compensable by different α_G.

## Section 5 — Weak Scale v/M_Pl (Electroweak Scale / Planck Scale)

### Identity

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Symbol | v / M_Pl |
| Name | Higgs vacuum expectation value / Planck mass |
| Measured value | v = 246 GeV, M_Pl = 1.22 × 10¹⁹ GeV → v/M_Pl ≈ 2 × 10⁻¹⁷ |
| Dimensionless? | Yes |
| Physical role | Determines W and Z boson masses (via m_W = gv/2), and thus the strength and range of the weak force. Also sets the electron and quark masses via Yukawa couplings. |
| Affected tiers | 1 (chemistry — neutron/proton ratio via weak interactions), 2 (stellar fusion — pp-chain weak reaction rates), 3 (supernova nucleosynthesis — r-process depends on weak rates), 4 (all of the above) |

### Life-Permitting Criterion

- **Tier 1 (stable chemistry):** The weak interaction determines the neutron lifetime (τ_n ~ 880 s at observed v). If v is much smaller, weak interactions are stronger, neutrons decay faster — affecting BBN and the n/p ratio. If v is much larger, weak interactions are weaker, affecting the pp-chain (which depends on p → n conversion via weak interactions).
- **Tier 2 (long-lived stars):** The proton-proton chain's first step (p + p → d + e⁺ + ν_e) is a weak interaction. If v/M_Pl changes by much, the pp rate changes dramatically, affecting stellar lifetimes and energy output.
- **Tier 3 (complex chemistry):** Supernova nucleosynthesis depends on weak interaction rates for r-process and s-process element production.

### Claimed Bounds

| Bound | Range | Tier | Source | Method | Confidence |
|-------|-------|------|--------|--------|------------|
| Upper (excessive weak force) | v/M_Pl < ~5 × 10⁻¹⁷ (upper) for BBN with n/p ratio viable | 1 | Agrawal et al. (1998) PRL 80, 1822 | BBN with varied v; weak rates computed | Moderate |
| Lower (weak force too weak) | v/M_Pl > ~10⁻¹⁸ (lower) for pp-chain to produce energy | 2 | Agrawal et al. (1998) | Stellar structure with varied weak rates | Moderate |
| Lower (carbon production) | v/M_Pl > 0.90 × (observed value) for appreciable C | 3 | Hogan (2000) citing Agrawal | Triple-alpha with varied v affecting nuclear binding | Weak (derived) |
| Upper (no weak interactions) | No constraint — Harnik et al. (2006) show life possible | 1–3 | Harnik, Kribs, Perez (2006) PRD 74, 035006 | "Weakless universe" — Standard Model without weak force | Weak (assumes compensation) |

**Critical meta-observation:** There are two very different conclusions in the literature:
1. **Agrawal et al. (1998):** The weak scale has a narrow anthropic window, v/M_Pl ∈ (10⁻¹⁸, 5 × 10⁻¹⁷), overlapping with the observed value. This is a moderate-confidence bound.
2. **Harnik et al. (2006):** A "weakless universe" — Standard Model with the weak interaction completely removed — could still be habitable. This suggests the weak scale is **not** anthropically constrained at all.

These two conclusions are contradictory. The resolution depends on whether alternative BBN and stellar fusion pathways (available without weak interactions) are considered "life-permitting."

### Metadata per Bound (Agrawal et al. 1998 — most cited)

- **What varied:** The Higgs mass parameter μ² (which sets v)
- **What held fixed:** All other constants
- **One-at-a-time:** Yes (with caveat that Yukawa couplings could also vary)
- **Definition of life-permitting:** "Life as we know it" — specifically:
  - BBN produces both protons and neutrons (not all one or other)
  - At least some stellar fusion possible
  - Sufficient carbon produced
- **Computational model:** Standard Model with varied v, including effects on:
  - Neutron-proton mass difference (~1.3 MeV in our universe)
  - Neutron lifetime
  - pp-chain reaction rates
  - Triple-alpha carbon production
- **Independence assumptions:** v treated as independent; Yukawa couplings (which set m_e, m_u, m_d) could vary independently but are held fixed

### Strongest Fine-Tuning Argument

**Agrawal et al. (1998):** The observed v/M_Pl (~2 × 10⁻¹⁷) sits within a narrow window ~(10⁻¹⁸, 5 × 10⁻¹⁷) where life is possible. Within this window, the observed value is "fairly typical" — not fine-tuned within the allowed range. This is notable: the weak scale is one of the few constants where the observed value appears *natural* under anthropic selection rather than requiring further tuning.

### Strongest Critique/Alternative

**Harnik, Kribs, Perez (2006):** A universe with *no* weak interactions (v → ∞, or zero Higgs vev for weak bosons) can still produce:

- Stable protons and neutrons (strong and EM forces suffice)
- Stars that fuse hydrogen via alternative pathways (direct p + p fusion without weak conversion still works if some neutrons exist)
- Heavy element production via supernovae with alternative cooling mechanisms

The paper concludes: "There is no anthropic requirement for a weak interaction." If correct, this completely undermines the Agrawal bound.

## Section 6 — Λ (Cosmological Constant)

### Identity

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Symbol | Λ |
| Name | Cosmological constant (in ΛCDM model) |
| Measured value | Λ ≈ 1.11 × 10⁻⁵² m⁻² → Λ M_Pl⁻² ≈ 1.0 × 10⁻¹²² (Planck units) |
| Dimensionless? | Yes, when expressed as ρ_Λ / M_Pl⁴ ≈ 10⁻¹²² or Λ M_Pl⁻² |
| Physical role | Energy density of the vacuum; drives accelerated cosmic expansion at late times |
| Affected tiers | 2 (galaxy formation — too much Λ prevents structure formation), 3 (star formation — needs galaxies), 4 (observers require galaxies) |

### Life-Permitting Criterion

- **Tier 2 (long-lived stars):** Λ must not be so large that cosmic expansion prevents the formation of gravitationally bound structures (galaxies, stars). If Λ is too large, it dominates the expansion before galaxies can form.
- **Tier 3 (complex chemistry):** Requires stars (from Tier 2) and therefore galaxies.
- **Tier 4 (information processing):** Requires planetary systems (Tier 3 + Tier 2).

### Claimed Bounds

| Bound | Range (Planck units) | Tier | Source | Method | Confidence |
|-------|---------------------|------|--------|--------|------------|
| Upper (galaxy formation) | Λ < ~10⁻¹¹⁸ (ρ_Λ < ~10⁻¹¹⁸ M_Pl⁴) | 2 | Weinberg (1987) PRL 59, 2607 | Structure formation: Λ dominates when ρ_m decays below Λ | Moderate |
| Upper (updated) | Λ < ~10⁻¹²⁰ (a few × observed) | 2 | Tegmark et al. (2006) PRD 74, 123507 | N-body simulations with varied Λ | Moderate |
| Upper (more permissive) | Λ < ~10⁻¹¹⁶ for *any* galaxies | 2 | Barnes (2012) arXiv:1112.4647 | Analytic: latest collapse time vs. Λ domination | Weak (depends on collapse model) |
| Lower | None — Λ = 0 is fine for life; no lower bound | 2 | Weinberg (1987) | A smaller Λ just means later Λ-domination | Firm |
| Observed vs. prediction | Λ_obs ≈ 10⁻¹²²; Weinberg upper bound = ~10⁻¹¹⁸ | — | — | Discrepancy: ~10⁴ × smaller than upper bound | — |

**Critical observation:** The observed Λ is ~10⁴ times smaller than the Weinberg (1987) anthropic upper bound. This means the anthropic principle, by itself, does **not** explain the smallness of Λ to high precision — it only predicts Λ ≲ 10⁻¹¹⁸, which is a much weaker constraint than the observed value.

### Metadata per Bound (Weinberg 1987 — foundational)

- **What varied:** Λ (taken as fundamental constant)
- **What held fixed:** All other constants, cosmological parameters (Ω_m, amplitude of fluctuations Q)
- **One-at-a-time:** Yes
- **Definition of life-permitting:** "Formation of gravitationally bound structures" (galaxies)
- **Computational model:** Linear perturbation theory in ΛCDM; Λ domination time vs. structure formation time
- **Independence assumptions:** Λ assumed independent of all other parameters; in string theory landscapes, Λ may correlate with other constants

### Strongest Fine-Tuning Argument

**Weinberg (1987) — the original:** The cosmological constant cannot be more than ~10⁻¹¹⁸ in Planck units, or galaxies would not form. Given that naive QFT estimates predict Λ ~ 1 in Planck units (the "cosmological constant problem"), the observed Λ is fine-tuned by ~120 orders of magnitude relative to QFT expectations.

**However:** The *anthropic* bound is only ~10⁴ times above the observed value. The 120 orders of magnitude is not an anthropic bound — it is the discrepancy between QFT and observation. The anthropic bound (Weinberg) is only ~4 orders of magnitude above observed. This is an important distinction often confused in popular presentations.

### Strongest Critique/Alternative

**Weinberg's own conclusion (1987):** "If so [if galaxy counts rule out Λ within 1-2 orders of magnitude of the upper bound], we may conclude that anthropic considerations do not explain the smallness of the cosmological constant." Weinberg himself was skeptical that anthropic reasoning could explain Λ.

**Adams (2019) §6:** Reviews structure formation simulations with varied Λ. If Λ is an order of magnitude larger than observed, galaxy formation is suppressed by ~50%. At 10× observed, almost no Milky-Way-like galaxies form. But at 0.1× observed, galaxies form *more* efficiently. There is no "lower bound" — smaller Λ is fine.

**Piran & Jimenez (2023):** "The cosmological constant does not need to be small for life to exist." Under alternative cosmic histories (e.g., different reionization scenarios), habitable planets could form even with Λ much larger than the Weinberg bound.

## Section 7 — Synthesis: Agreement, Model-Dependence, and Disagreement

### Level of Agreement Across Sources

| Constant | Number of Independent Bounds | Agreement Level | Key Papers |
|----------|----------------------------|-----------------|------------|
| α | 3+ distinct bounds (Tier 1, 2, 3) | Good — bounds differ by tier, not contradiction | Barrow & Tipler (1986); Livio et al. (1989); Adams (2019) |

### Where the Disagreements Come From

1. **Definition of "life-permitting":** The Tier 2 vs. Tier 3 distinction explains nearly all disagreements over α and αs. Bounds are an order of magnitude wider when only "stars exist" (Tier 2) is required vs. "carbon exists" (Tier 3).

2. **Model choice for stellar physics:** The "diproton catastrophe" disagreement (αs) arises from different BBN models. Davies (1993) assumed complete hydrogen consumption; MacDonald & Mullan (2009) used a full BBN network and found otherwise.

3. **Alternative life chemistry:** Harnik et al. (2006) challenge the assumption that life requires the weak interaction at all. If alternative biochemistries are considered anthropically relevant, bounds on weak-scale parameters vanish entirely.

4. **One-at-a-time vs. simultaneous variation:** Almost all bounds assume one-at-a-time variation. If constants can vary jointly (as in many string landscape scenarios), the effective anthropic constraints may be much weaker or much stronger depending on correlations.

5. **The reference class problem:** Which parameters are allowed to vary? Adams (2019) treats 26 Standard Model parameters plus cosmological parameters. Some analyses vary only 6 "fundamental" constants. The number of independent parameters changes which bounds are narrow.

### What Remains Genuinely Unknown

1. **μ = m_e/m_p (electron-proton mass ratio):** No quantitative anthropic bound exists in the peer-reviewed literature. The claim that this ratio is "fine-tuned" appears in popular books but lacks a specific published calculation of the life-permitting range. This is the **most gaping hole** in the fine-tuning literature.

2. **Joint variation of (α, αs):** While individually bounded to ~4% and ~0.5% respectively, their joint correlation (expected in grand unified theories) could make the effective bound much narrower or wider depending on how they co-vary. Few papers explore this.

3. **Weak scale with correlated Yukawas:** Agrawal et al. vary v while holding Yukawa couplings fixed. But if v changes in a more complete theory, the Yukawa couplings (and thus fermion masses) change too, potentially widening the allowed range.
