# T600.1 — Surviving Claim Inventory

## Mission

Catalog every claim that survived T500 with exact wording,
confidence level, supporting evidence, opposing evidence,
and the specific new evidence that would change the verdict.

---

## Claim 1: Λ has a genuine anthropic upper bound for structure formation

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Exact claim** | The cosmological constant Λ must be less than approximately 10⁻¹¹⁶ (Planck units) for gravitationally bound structures (galaxies) to form. |
| **Current confidence** | **HIGH** |
| **Evidence supporting** | Weinberg (1987) analytic perturbation theory; Tegmark et al. (2006) N-body ΛCDM simulation; Barnes (2012) independent analytic review; Piran & Jimenez (2023) N-body simulation. Four independent calculations agree on the existence of an upper bound. |
| **Evidence against** | The exact bound value varies across calculations by ~10⁴× (10⁻¹¹⁶ to 10⁻¹²⁰). Piran & Jimenez (2023) find factor ~100 variation still allows galaxies. The definition of "galaxy formation sufficient for life" is not unique. |
| **New evidence that would change confidence** | An N-body simulation showing galaxies form at Λ > 10⁻¹¹⁰ would lower confidence to MODERATE. A fully coupled galaxy-formation + star-formation simulation showing life requires Λ < 10⁻¹²⁵ would raise it. |
| **Attack that would falsify** | Show that a universe with Λ > 10⁻¹¹⁰ still forms gravitationally bound structures with stars and planets. |

## Claim 2: Observer selection explains the observed Λ value

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Exact claim** | The observed value Λ = 10⁻¹²² is within the life-permitting range; observer selection (we could not observe otherwise) is a complete alternative explanation for why Λ is small. |
| **Current confidence** | **MODERATE** |
| **Evidence supporting** | Carter (1974) weak anthropic principle; Bostrom (2002) observer selection effect formalization; Garriga & Vilenkin (2003) anthropic prediction gives Λ ≈ 0.7 × Λ_anth ≈ 7 × 10⁻¹¹⁹, correct to ~4 orders of magnitude. Srednicki & Hartle (2010) causal-patch measure gives peaked distribution near observed Λ. |
| **Evidence against** | Garriga-Vilenkin prediction has factor ~3000× discrepancy with observed Λ. OSE cannot explain why α = 1/137 specifically. The reference class problem (what counts as an observer?) is unresolved. |
| **New evidence that would change confidence** | A multiverse prediction for Λ with accuracy within 10% would raise confidence. A non-anthropic fundamental physics explanation for Λ (e.g., a symmetry or screening mechanism) would make OSE unnecessary. |
| **Attack that would falsify** | Show that observer selection fails to predict Λ within an order of magnitude, and no multiverse measure gives Λ near the observed value. |

## Claim 3: Λ is fine-tuned (relative to QFT natural expectation)

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Exact claim** | The observed Λ = 10⁻¹²² is ~10¹²² below the natural QFT expectation (Λ_QFT ~ 1 in Planck units). |
| **Current confidence** | **LOW** (this is a problem for QFT, not evidence for fine-tuning) |
| **Evidence supporting** | The naive QFT vacuum energy calculation gives Λ ~ M_Pl⁴ ~ 1. The observed value is ~120 orders of magnitude smaller. This is the largest discrepancy between theory and observation in physics. |
| **Evidence against** | The QFT calculation is known to be unreliable — renormalization absorbs the bare cosmological constant. Supersymmetry (if it exists) would cancel the leading contributions. The discrepancy is a problem for QFT quantum gravity, not evidence about life. |
| **New evidence that would change confidence** | A reliable non-perturbative QFT calculation confirming the naive Λ expectation would raise confidence. A demonstration that SUSY cancels Λ naturally would lower it (making fine-tuning unnecessary). |
| **Attack that would falsify** | Show that the QFT Λ expectation is not physically meaningful — that the discrepancy is an artifact of applying effective field theory beyond its domain of validity. |

## Claim 4: α and αs have narrow (few-percent) bounds for carbon production

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Exact claim** | The fine-structure constant α must be within ±4% and the strong coupling αs within ±0.5% of their observed values for carbon production in stellar nucleosynthesis (C/O ratio > 0.1). |
| **Current confidence** | **LOW** |
| **Evidence supporting** | Livio et al. (1989) Nature 340, 281–284: stellar nucleosynthesis calculation with varied α and αs. Oberhummer et al. (2000) Science 289, 88–90: microscopic nuclear model confirming αs sensitivity. |
| **Evidence against** | **No independent replication in 36 years.** Livio's code was proprietary. The bound is carbon-only (silicon life would not require the Hoyle state). The C/O > 0.1 threshold is arbitrary. Joint α-αs variation was not explored. |
| **New evidence that would change confidence** | An independent MESA simulation reproducing the ±4% and ±0.5% bounds would raise to MODERATE. A calculation showing silicon-based life exceeds the C/O criterion in complexity would further lower (the bound is already LOW). |
| **Attack that would falsify** | An independent MESA simulation showing C/O > 0.1 at Δα/α = ±10% would falsify. A published chemical analysis showing silicon self-replication is feasible would make the carbon bound irrelevant. |

## Claim 5: μ (m_e/m_p) has a fundamental lower bound of m_p > m_e

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Exact claim** | The proton must be heavier than the electron for stable atomic bound states to exist. |
| **Current confidence** | **HIGH** |
| **Evidence supporting** | Standard QED: if m_e > m_p, the electron would be the nucleus and the proton the orbiting particle. The Bohr radius and binding energies invert. No stable atoms of the known type would form. |
| **Evidence against** | The exact threshold where m_p ≈ m_e still permits bound states is non-perturbative and estimated only. Exotic bound states (e.g., positronium-like) could exist if m_p ≈ m_e. |
| **New evidence that would change confidence** | A rigorous non-perturbative QED calculation showing bound states survive at m_p ~ m_e would lower confidence. |
| **Attack that would falsify** | Show that stable atoms exist when m_e > m_p (e.g., "inverted" atoms where the proton orbits the electron). |

## Claim 6: μ has a Tier 3 bound of approximately factor ~100

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Exact claim** | The electron-proton mass ratio μ = m_e/m_p must lie within approximately a factor of 100 of its observed value (Adams 2019: μ ∈ [5.45×10⁻⁵, 0.00545]) for complex chemistry. |
| **Current confidence** | **LOW** |
| **Evidence supporting** | Carr & Rees (1979) order-of-magnitude estimates; Adams (2019) review compilation. If μ were much larger, molecular bonds would be too strong for metabolism. If μ were much smaller, electron wavefunctions would be too diffuse. |
| **Evidence against** | **No simulation has been performed.** The factor ~100 bound is an analytic estimate, not a computation. No T3 bound exists — the effect of varied μ on chemical reaction networks has not been studied. |
| **New evidence that would change confidence** | Quantum chemistry simulations (Gaussian, NWChem) with varied m_e/m_p showing reaction networks fail outside a narrower range would raise confidence. A demonstration that large-μ chemistry works would lower it. |
| **Attack that would falsify** | Quantum chemistry calculation showing complex organic molecules form at μ 10× larger or smaller than observed. |

## Claim 7: αs has a BBN bound of ±50%

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Exact claim** | The strong coupling constant must be within approximately ±50% of its observed value for stable hydrogen (survival of the diproton bound) during Big Bang nucleosynthesis. |
| **Current confidence** | **MODERATE** |
| **Evidence supporting** | MacDonald & Mullan (2009) Phys. Rev. D 80, 043507: full BBN network with varied αs. Refutes the narrower Davies (1993) bound. Standard BBN physics is well-understood. |
| **Evidence against** | Not independently replicated with a different BBN code. Adams (2019) compiles a ±0.5% bound for ⁴He mass ordering (a different constraint). |
| **New evidence that would change confidence** | An independent BBN code (Parthenope, AlterBBN) reproducing the ±50% result would raise to HIGH. A demonstration that even ±100% still allows hydrogen would lower further. |
| **Attack that would falsify** | Show that hydrogen survives at Δαs/αs = ±100% in an independent BBN code. |

## Claim 8: αG has a stellar structure bound of factor ~100

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Exact claim** | The gravitational fine-structure constant α_G must be within approximately a factor of 100 (Adams 2019: α_G ∈ [5.9×10⁻⁴⁰, 5.9×10⁻³⁸]) for stars to have lifetimes > 1 Gyr. |
| **Current confidence** | **MODERATE** (stellar bound exists); **NONE** (life bound) |
| **Evidence supporting** | Carr & Rees (1979) analytic scaling relations; Adams (2019) modern stellar models. Standard stellar structure physics — Jeans mass, Chandrasekhar mass, stellar cooling times. |
| **Evidence against** | The bound is for stellar lifetimes, not for life. No T3 bound exists (αG affects star formation but not direct chemistry). The factor ~100 is wide. |
| **New evidence that would change confidence** | A demonstration that life requires stars with specific masses that αG constrains would raise to LOW. A published alternative energy source for life (e.g., geothermal on a gas giant) would make this irrelevant. |
| **Attack that would falsify** | Show that life can exist without stars (geothermal energy, radioactive decay, tidal heating). |

## Claim 9: No valid probability measure exists for fine-tuning

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Exact claim** | Fine-tuning probabilities cannot be meaningfully computed because no physically justified probability measure over fundamental constants exists. |
| **Current confidence** | **HIGH** |
| **Evidence supporting** | Five distinct measures (M1–M5) give results spanning 10⁴⁰× for the same physical scenario (T300.6C). 59% of sources use no measure. The normalization problem (McGrew et al. 2001) — infinite ranges give zero or ill-defined probabilities. |
| **Evidence against** | Barnes (2012) argues that sensitivity analysis over prior ranges shows robustness. The ratio-based (log-uniform) approach gives stable order-of-magnitude estimates for some constants. |
| **New evidence that would change confidence** | A physically derived measure from a fundamental theory (e.g., string theory measure fix) would lower confidence. An impossibility proof (measure problem is mathematically undecidable) would raise it. |
| **Attack that would falsify** | Derive a unique, physically justified probability measure over fundamental constants from a fundamental theory. |

## Claim 10: Observer selection fully accounts for what we observe

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Exact claim** | Observer selection effects (we can only observe universes compatible with our existence) provide a complete and sufficient explanation for why fundamental constants fall within life-permitting ranges. |
| **Current confidence** | **MODERATE** |
| **Evidence supporting** | Carter (1974) WAP; Bostrom (2002) OSE formalization; Garriga-Vilenkin (2003) Λ prediction within ~4 orders of magnitude; Srednicki-Hartle (2010) causal-patch measure. |
| **Evidence against** | Cannot predict specific values (why α = 1/137, not 1/128?). The reference class problem is unresolved. The Boltzmann brain problem. For Λ, the factor ~3000× discrepancy. |
| **New evidence that would change confidence** | A non-anthropic derivation of even one constant would undermine OSE (but not defeat it). A confirmed multiverse observation (e.g., bubble collision in CMB) would support OSE indirectly. |
| **Attack that would falsify** | Show that the observed constants are not typical among life-permitting values (the typicality problem). |
