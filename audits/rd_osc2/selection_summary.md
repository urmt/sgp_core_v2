## Potential Biases

1. **Late-archive overrepresentation**: 11 of 20 studies are from RD-10B. This reflects the concentration of explanatory locus data in the late archive (38 of 41 loci are RD-series). The early archive has fewer studies with locus data, creating structural bias.

2. **Persistence dominance**: 7 Persistence studies selected (35%). This mirrors the archive's actual distribution (14 of 41 = 34%), so it is proportionally accurate but may overrepresent persistence-type oscillation patterns.

3. **NO-gain underrepresentation**: Only 2 studies with NO gain after comparison (10%). The archive has 4 such studies (10%), so this is proportionally accurate but limits the ability to detect oscillation in non-gaining studies.

4. **Transformation rarity**: Only 1 Transformation study selected. The archive has only 2 Transformation studies total, so this is unavoidable.

5. **Decomposition-decomposition gap**: No decomposition-decomposition interaction type studies selected. The archive has 3 such studies (RD-10B.J6, J7, J8), all of which are persistence-locus. They were deprioritized in favor of more diverse locus types.

6. **Series concentration**: All studies are from T, Phase, or RD series. The scripts series (62 studies) has no explanatory locus data and was excluded entirely. This is a structural gap — scripts studies may oscillate differently.

## What Blind Coders Will See

Each packet contains ONLY:
- A blind identifier (S01–S20)
- A description of what the study did (no labels)
- Beginning state
- End state
- Key numerical findings (if any)

Coders will NOT see:
- Explanatory locus type
- Persistence type
- Whether gain occurred after comparison
- Interaction type
- Any previous classification

The audit tests whether blind coders can detect oscillation patterns (recurring returns to similar states) without knowledge of the study's position or classification.
