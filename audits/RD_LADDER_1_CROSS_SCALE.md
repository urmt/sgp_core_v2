# RD-LADDER.1 — Cross-Scale Persistence Reconstruction

**Question:** What operation is present at every successful level of the compositional ladder? What additional operation first appears at each level?

**Method:** Direct reconstruction of each rung. No ontology. No clustering. No coding categories. Just what interacts, what persists, and what allows persistence.

---

## Rung 1: Photon

**What interacts:**
- Photon interacts with electromagnetic field
- Photon can convert to electron-positron pair near nucleus
- Photon absorbed or emitted during atomic transitions

**What persists:**
- Energy (conserved)
- Momentum (conserved)
- Spin angular momentum (conserved)
- Polarization state (can persist through propagation)

**What allows persistence:**
- Conservation laws (energy, momentum, angular momentum)
- Photon is excitation of electromagnetic field; field persists even when photon is absorbed

**What new persistence appears:**
- Polarization state: a binary property that can persist through interactions

**What lower-level persistence is preserved:**
- None (first rung)

---

## Rung 2: Electron (Quantum Particle)

**What interacts:**
- Electron interacts with electromagnetic field (via photon exchange)
- Electron interacts with other electrons (Coulomb repulsion)
- Electron interacts with protons (bound states)
- Electron obeys Pauli exclusion principle

**What persists:**
- Electric charge (conserved)
- Spin-1/2 (conserved)
- Lepton number (conserved)
- Bound state energy levels (quantized, stable)

**What allows persistence:**
- Quantization: energy levels are discrete, transitions require exact energy
- Pauli exclusion: prevents collapse into same state
- Shielding: inner electrons shield outer electrons from nucleus

**What new persistence appears:**
- Bound state: electron can be trapped in potential well, persists unless energy input exceeds binding energy
- Quantized energy levels: discrete states that persist

**What lower-level persistence is preserved:**
- Energy conservation (from photon)
- Momentum conservation (from photon)
- Angular momentum conservation (from photon)

---

## Rung 3: Atom

**What interacts:**
- Nucleus interacts with electrons (electromagnetic)
- Electrons interact with each other (Coulomb)
- Atom interacts with other atoms (van der Waals, covalent, ionic bonds)
- Atom interacts with light (absorption, emission, scattering)

**What persists:**
- Electron shell structure (determined by quantum numbers)
- Chemical identity (determined by proton number)
- Ionization energy (energy required to remove electron)
- Excited states (metastable states with lifetimes > 10^-8 s)

**What allows persistence:**
- Shell structure: filled shells are particularly stable
- Electron-electron repulsion prevents further compression
- Nuclear binding energy (MeV scale) far exceeds chemical binding energy (eV scale)

**What new persistence appears:**
- Chemical identity: atom with N protons is always the same element
- Shell structure: periodic table emerges from quantum numbers
- Metastable states: excited states that persist for measurable times

**What lower-level persistence is preserved:**
- Quantized energy levels (from electron)
- Spin (from electron)
- Charge conservation (from electron)
- Exclusion principle (from electron)

---

## Rung 4: Molecule

**What interacts:**
- Atoms interact via chemical bonds (covalent, ionic, metallic)
- Molecules interact via intermolecular forces (van der Waals, hydrogen bonding)
- Molecules interact with light (vibrational, rotational, electronic transitions)
- Molecules interact with enzymes (catalysis)

**What persists:**
- Molecular structure (3D geometry)
- Bond lengths and angles (determined by electron orbitals)
- Functional groups (reactive sites)
- Molecular chirality (handedness)

**What allows persistence:**
- Covalent bonds: shared electrons create stable configurations
- Bond energies: typically 1-10 eV, requiring significant energy to break
- Molecular orbitals: electrons delocalized across multiple atoms

**What new persistence appears:**
- 3D structure: molecule has shape, not just composition
- Chirality: molecule can be left- or right-handed (mirror images not superimposable)
- Functional groups: specific arrangements of atoms with specific reactivity

**What lower-level persistence is preserved:**
- Electron shell structure (from atom)
- Chemical identity (from atom)
- Quantized energy levels (from atom)
- Spin (from electron)

---

## Rung 5: Autocatalytic Chemistry

**What interacts:**
- Molecules catalyze reactions that produce more of themselves
- Autocatalytic sets: networks where each reaction is catalyzed by another member
- Competition for shared resources
- Cross-inhibition between autocatalytic sets

**What persists:**
- Autocatalytic cycle (self-sustaining reaction network)
- Information (which molecules are needed)
- Template molecules (that direct synthesis of copies)

**What allows persistence:**
- Catalysis: lowers activation energy, speeds up reactions
- Template-directed synthesis: molecule directs production of copy
- Compartmentalization: vesicles concentrate reactants

**What new persistence appears:**
- Self-sustaining reaction: cycle continues without external energy input (as long as raw materials available)
- Template: molecule contains information that directs its own replication
- Competition: different autocatalytic sets compete for resources

**What lower-level persistence is preserved:**
- Molecular structure (from molecule)
- Bond energies (from molecule)
- Chirality (from molecule)
- Chemical identity (from atom)

---

## Rung 6: Cell

**What interacts:**
- Lipid membrane separates inside from outside
- DNA stores information
- RNA transcribes and translates information
- Proteins perform functions
- Metabolism provides energy

**What persists:**
- Genome (DNA sequence)
- Cell membrane (lipid bilayer)
- Metabolic pathways
- Gene regulatory networks

**What allows persistence:**
- DNA replication: high-fidelity copying (error rate ~10^-9 per base pair per generation)
- DNA repair: multiple mechanisms correct errors
- Cell division: membrane, organelles, and genome are copied
- Homeostasis: cell maintains internal conditions

**What new persistence appears:**
- Genome: information that directs development of entire organism
- Heredity: information passed from parent to offspring
- Evolution: variation + selection = adaptation over generations
- Death: cell can undergo programmed death (apoptosis)

**What lower-level persistence is preserved:**
- Autocatalytic cycles (from autocatalytic chemistry)
- Template-directed synthesis (from autocatalytic chemistry)
- Molecular structure (from molecule)
- Chemical identity (from atom)

---

## Rung 7: Multicellular Organism

**What interacts:**
- Cells interact via signaling molecules (hormones, growth factors)
- Cells differentiate into tissues and organs
- Immune system distinguishes self from non-self
- Germline cells preserve genome across generations

**What persists:**
- Body plan (developmental program)
- Tissue architecture
- Organ function
- Germ line (genome passed to offspring)

**What allows persistence:**
- Cell adhesion: cells stick together to form tissues
- Cell signaling: cells communicate to coordinate behavior
- Apoptosis: programmed cell death removes damaged cells
- Stem cells: undifferentiated cells replace worn-out cells

**What new persistence appears:**
- Tissue: group of cells with shared function
- Organ: group of tissues with shared function
- Body plan: spatial arrangement of tissues and organs
- Germ-soma distinction: germline persists, soma dies

**What lower-level persistence is preserved:**
- Genome (from cell)
- Cell membrane (from cell)
- Metabolic pathways (from cell)
- Heredity (from cell)

---

## Rung 8: Nervous System

**What interacts:**
- Neurons interact via synapses (chemical and electrical)
- Sensory neurons detect environment
- Motor neurons control muscles
- Interneurons process information

**What persists:**
- Neural circuits (patterns of connectivity)
- Synaptic strengths (learning)
- Neural oscillations (rhythmic activity)
- Reflexes (innate responses)

**What allows persistence:**
- Synaptic plasticity: connection strengths change based on activity
- Long-term potentiation: repeated stimulation strengthens synapses
- Myelin: insulation speeds signal transmission
- Refractory period: neurons cannot fire immediately after firing

**What new persistence appears:**
- Learning: experience changes synaptic strengths
- Memory: information persists in synaptic connections
- Reflexes: hard-wired responses to specific stimuli
- Neural oscillations: rhythmic activity coordinates neural populations

**What lower-level persistence is preserved:**
- Body plan (from multicellular organism)
- Tissue architecture (from multicellular organism)
- Genome (from cell)
- Cell membrane (from cell)

---

## Rung 9: Mind

**What interacts:**
- Brain interacts with body (sensory input, motor output)
- Brain interacts with environment (perception, action)
- Brain interacts with other brains (communication, social interaction)
- Brain generates internal models (prediction, imagination)

**What persists:**
- Self-model (model of own body and capabilities)
- World-model (model of environment)
- Goals (desired states)
- Preferences (learned valuations)

**What allows persistence:**
- Consolidation: important memories strengthened during sleep
- Reinforcement: rewarding experiences strengthen behaviors
- Punishment: aversive experiences weaken behaviors
- Social learning: observations of others stored as memories

**What new persistence appears:**
- Self-model: brain generates model of itself as agent
- World-model: brain generates model of environment as structured
- Goals: brain generates desired future states
- Language: arbitrary symbols represent concepts, enabling abstract reasoning

**What lower-level persistence is preserved:**
- Neural circuits (from nervous system)
- Synaptic strengths (from nervous system)
- Learning (from nervous system)
- Memory (from nervous system)
- Body plan (from multicellular organism)
- Genome (from cell)

## Standing Observation

**Interaction is present at every rung.**

Every level involves components interacting with each other and with the environment.

**Persistence requires something that resists dissolution.**

At each level, persistence is enabled by a specific mechanism:
- Conservation laws (photon)
- Quantization and exclusion (electron)
- Shell structure (atom)
- Covalent bonds (molecule)
- Catalysis and templates (autocatalytic)
- DNA replication and repair (cell)
- Cell adhesion and signaling (multicellular)
- Synaptic plasticity (nervous)
- Consolidation and reinforcement (mind)

**Each level preserves lower-level persistence while adding new persistence.**

The ladder is cumulative: each rung includes everything below it plus something new.
