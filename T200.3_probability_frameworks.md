**Critical caveat:** For the linear measure, constants with very small observed values (α_G ~10⁻³⁹, Λ ~10⁻¹²²) automatically receive near-zero probability regardless of anthropic bounds, because almost all the prior volume lies at O(1). This is a well-known pathology of the linear measure.

### 2.2 Sensitivity to Prior Range Choice

For the log-uniform measure, changing the total prior range by ±X decades changes every probability by a multiplicative factor of 120/(120 ± X). This is a minor effect (<2×) for modest changes. For the linear measure, changing the upper bound from 1 to 10 changes probabilities by 10× — the linear measure is pathologically prior-dominated.

## 4. The Log-Linear Pathology

The linear measure produces probabilities that are **artifacts of the prior range choice** for constants with small values. If you force constants like α_G (~10⁻³⁹) to share a prior range [0, 1] with O(1) constants, it appears "infinitely improbable" regardless of the anthropic bounds. This is a measure artifact — not physics.

**Quantification:**

| Constant | Linear P (any bound) | Log P | Ratio | Source of ratio |
|----------|---------------------|-------|-------|-----------------|
| α (T1) | 0.099 | 0.017 | 5.8× | Constant is O(0.01), mild effect |
| αs (T1 narrow) | 0.060 | 0.0025 | 24× | Constant is O(0.1), mild effect |
| α_G (T2) | ~10⁻³⁸ | 0.017 | **10³⁶×** | Value is 10⁻³⁹, pathology |
| v/M_Pl (T1) | ~10⁻¹⁷ | 0.014 | **10¹⁵×** | Value is 10⁻¹⁷, pathology |
| Λ (T2) | ~10⁻¹¹⁸ | 0.048 | **10¹¹⁶×** | Value is 10⁻¹²², pathology |

The three "improbable" constants (α_G, v/M_Pl, Λ) appear improbable under the linear measure only because the measure forces them to share a [0, 1] range with other constants — not because their anthropic bounds are narrow.

**Conclusion:** The linear measure is unusable for any constant spanning more than ~2 orders of magnitude below the prior upper bound. Results from the linear measure should be interpreted as "this constant's value is far from the Planck scale" — which is a fact about naturalness, not about anthropic fine-tuning.

### 5.3 Model C: Propagated Through Coupled Subsystems

Model C explicitly propagates the coupled subsystem. For each tier, the coupled subsystem (αs–μ–α_G) is treated as a joint distribution with known covariances.

For the αs–μ–α_G subsystem:
- αs determines Λ_QCD via Λ_QCD ≈ M_Pl exp(−2π/(β₀ αs(M_Pl)))
- m_p = f × Λ_QCD (f ∼ few)
- μ = m_e / m_p = y_e · v · Λ_QCD⁻¹
- α_G = G m_p² / (ħc) = G f² Λ_QCD² / (ħc)

If αs varies and all other SM parameters (y_e, v, G) are held fixed:
- μ ∝ Λ_QCD⁻¹ ∝ exp(2π/(β₀ αs)) — exponentially sensitive
- α_G ∝ Λ_QCD² ∝ exp(−4π/(β₀ αs)) — exponentially sensitive
- μ ∝ α_G⁻¹/² — anti-correlated

The effective anthropic constraint is: what range of αs is life-permitting for this subsystem?

For Tier 3, the αs bound (0.5% for carbon) is already the most restrictive and would dominate. The α_G and μ bounds are automatically inherited once αs is fixed.

Model C thus converges to Model B for the coupled subsystem — the most restrictive bound within the group dominates, and the wider bounds are automatically satisfied.

**Key result:** Models B and C give similar results because the subsystem's constraints are hierarchical: one bound (αs for carbon, α_G for stellar lifetimes) is always the most restrictive, and the others are automatically satisfied within that range.

The true distinction between 6-independent (Model A) and dependency-corrected (Models B/C) is:
- **Under Model A:** αs AND μ AND α_G each contribute separate improbability → product is ~10³× smaller
- **Under Model B/C:** αs drives the subsystem → 1 contribution is enough

### 6.4 Observer-Selection Uncertainty (O)

No quantitative observer-selection correction is attempted. The observer-weighted measure (C) requires N_obs(x) which is not computable with current understanding of:
- What constitutes an "observer"
- How observer density varies with fundamental constants
- Whether observers exist elsewhere in the parameter space

**Uncertainty from observer selection is unbounded in both directions.** It could make fine-tuned parameters seem natural (if there are many observers at the observed value) or more extreme (if the observer density is low everywhere else).

2. **v/M_Pl constraint (D_v):** Factor ∞ depending on whether Harnik is correct
3. **Measure choice (M):** Factor 10¹¹⁶ for constants with small values
4. **αs range (R_αs):** Factor ~2 (contested bound)
5. **Range and measure for μ (R_μ, M_μ):** Factor ∞ (no bound)

## 8. Answer to the Primary Question

> *"How much of the reported fine-tuning signal survives after correcting for measure ambiguity, dependency structure, and uncertainty in anthropic bounds?"*

**Short answer:** The surviving signal is concentrated in two specific bounds — αs for carbon (Livio 0.5%) and α for carbon (Livio 4%) — under Tier 3 definition of "life-permitting." Everything else either:

1. **Has no bound** (μ) → contributes zero fine-tuning signal
2. **Is fundamentally contested** (v/M_Pl) → signal is either moderate (Agrawal) or zero (Harnik)
3. **Is prior-range-dominated** (α_G, Λ, v/M_Pl under linear measure) → the "improbability" is a measure artifact
4. **Is wider than the narrowest bounds** (α_G for T3 is factor 100 → 0.033 under log, much more permissive than αs at 3.7×10⁻⁵)

The maximum credible fine-tuning signal (Model B, log measure, Tier 3, excluding μ) is approximately **1 part in 10¹²** — not 1 part in 10¹⁰⁰ or 10¹²⁰ as commonly quoted.

If Harnik is correct about the weak scale, or if alternative biochemistries are admitted, the signal drops to zero (or is uncomputable).

If observer-selection effects are considered, the direction of the correction is unknown — it could make the odds larger, smaller, or eliminate them entirely.

## 9. What the Matrix Reveals

1. **The 10¹²⁰ number is wrong for two reasons:** It conflates the Weinberg anthropic bound (factor ~10⁴ above observed) with the QFT naturalness problem (factor ~10¹²⁰). Only ~4 orders of magnitude are anthropically relevant.

2. **The α–αs–μ triad cannot sustain probability multiplication.** Under dependency correction, the 0.5% αs bound dominates the subsystem, and μ contributes nothing (no bound). Popular estimates that quote "α to 4%, αs to 0.5%, μ to 1%" and multiply them are committing a double error: (a) μ has no bound, and (b) αs determines μ anyway.

3. **The log-uniform measure is the only defensible choice.** The linear measure is pathologically dominated by the prior upper bound for any constant with a small observed value.

4. **The Λ "fine-tuning" is primarily a naturalness problem, not an anthropic one.** The anthropically relevant range (Weinberg 1987) gives only ~10⁴ improvement over the observed value. The 120-orders-of-magnitude quote compares QFT expectations to observation — not an anthropic bound.

5. **Observer selection is the largest uncapped uncertainty.** The agnostic measure (D) is in many ways the most honest: we cannot assign a well-defined probability because the reference class of "possible universes" and the distribution of observers within them are both unknown.
