# T600.3 — Carbon Chauvinism Audit

## Mission

Evaluate whether the T500 conclusions that depend specifically on
carbon-based life are defensible after new evidence.

---

## The Carbon Chauvinism Question

The T500 analysis assumed that alternative biochemistries (silicon,
ammonia, exotic) could plausibly support life, which made the
carbon-specific α/αs bounds irrelevant. This assumption was based
on Bains (2004) and similar speculative proposals.

**Critical update:** Petkowski, Bains, Seager (2020) *Life* 10, 84
comprehensively reviewed organosilicon chemistry and concluded:
"In no environment is a life based primarily around silicon
chemistry a plausible option."

Since Bains is a co-author of the 2020 paper, this represents
a retraction by the original proponent of silicon-based life.

---

## Alternatives Evaluated

### Silicon

| Property | Assessment | Source |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| Bond diversity | Si-Si and Si-C bonds weak; Si=O double bonds unstable | Petkowski (2020) |
| Solubility in water | Silica (SiO₂) formation removes Si from solution | Petkowski (2020) |
| Solubility in cryosolvents | All molecules insoluble at cryogenic temperatures | Petkowski (2020) |
| Solubility in H₂SO₄ | Some organosilicon diversity; not a building block | Petkowski (2020) |
| Polymer formation | Si-O chains (silicones) too stable for metabolism | Mixed assessment |
| Information storage | Si-based polymers lack replication mechanism | No evidence exists |

**Verdict:** Silicon-based life is NOT chemically plausible in any
known solvent. The "silicon life escape hatch" is closed.

### Ammonia

| Property | Assessment | Source |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| Solvent properties | Good organic solvent; liquid down to -78°C | Schulze-Makuch (2006) |
| Carbon chemistry | Carbon-based molecules dissolve well in ammonia | General chemistry |
| Polymerization | Possible but slower at low temperatures | Speculative |
| Energy metabolism | Possible but low reaction rates | Speculative |

**Verdict:** Ammonia could serve as an alternative solvent, but
the chemistry would still be carbon-based. This does NOT eliminate
the α/αs bounds — it rearranges them around different temperature
sensitivities. Ammonia-based life would still require carbon, hence
the Hoyle state and α/αs constraints still apply.

### Methane / Cryosolvents

| Property | Assessment | Source |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| Solubility | Extremely low for all biomolecules | Benner (2010) |
| Reaction rates | Near-zero at cryogenic temperatures | General chemistry |
| Energy availability | Minimal chemical energy at low T | Speculative |

**Verdict:** Cryosolvents (liquid N₂, CH₄) are poor environments
for any known biochemistry. Cannot support life as understood.

### Plasma / Dark Matter / Exotic Life

| Property | Assessment | Source |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| Coherence | No mechanism for replication in plasma | Speculative |
| Information | No known information storage mechanism | Speculative |
| Evolution | No mechanism for Darwinian evolution | Speculative |

**Verdict:** These proposals lack any published chemical or
physical mechanism. They are speculation, not science.

---

## Information-Processing Criteria

An alternative approach: define life by information processing,
not chemistry (Walker & Davies 2017 *Interface Focus* 7, 20170038).

If life requires information storage and replication:
- Carbon provides the maximum chemical information density known
- Silicon's lower bond diversity means lower information density
- No alternative provides equivalent information capacity

**Consequence:** Carbon may be necessary for complex life even
under information-theoretic criteria. This is a philosophical
argument, not a physics result.

## Conclusion

**The T500 analysis over-weighted Bains (2004) and under-weighted
the comprehensive Petkowski et al. (2020) refutation.** The 2020
paper provides strong evidence that carbon is the only chemically
feasible substrate for complex life in known solvents. This
significantly weakens the "alternative life destroys α/αs bounds"
argument used in T500.

**However:** Even with carbon as the unique substrate, the α/αs
bounds are still unreplicated single-paper results from 1989.
The Huang (2019) MESA simulation widened the allowed range by
~3×. The fine-tuning claim for α/αs is strengthened in principle
(carbon is relevant) but weakened in practice (bounds are wider
than thought).

**Net effect:** The T500 conclusion that α/αs fine-tuning claims
are WEAK remains correct, but the reasoning must be updated.
The claim is weak because the bound is unreplicated and wider
than recorded, not because alternative life exists.
