# T600.4 — Measure Impossibility Audit

## Mission

Determine whether any probability statement about fine-tuning is
mathematically defensible after adversarial review. Classify
probability claims as Defensible, Weak, or Non-computable.

---

## The Problem: Why Fine-Tuning Needs a Measure

A "fine-tuning" claim implicitly asserts P(life | constants = X) is
small. This requires:

1. A parameter space Ω over which constants can vary
2. A probability measure μ on Ω
3. A "life-permitting" subset L ⊂ Ω
4. Computation of P(L) = ∫_L dμ / ∫_Ω dμ

Each step has fundamental difficulties.

---

## Classification of Probability Claims

### Claim Type 1: Ratio of Widths

**What it is:** "α must be within ±4% of its value, so its
fine-tuning is 1/25 = 4%."

**Assessment: NON-COMPUTABLE as a probability.**

This is a RATIO OF WIDTHS, not a probability. A ratio of widths
only becomes a probability if:
- The underlying measure is uniform over the parameter
- The parameter range is bounded
- No other constants affect the probability

**Why it fails:**
- No physical justification for uniformity
- No natural bound on the range of α (would be some
  arbitrarily chosen upper/lower cutoff)
- McGrew, McGrew & Vestrup (2001): infinite ranges give
  non-normalizable measures → no probability

**Verdict:** Ratios of widths are NOT probabilities. They are
dimensionless numbers with no frequency interpretation.

---

### Claim Type 2: Log-Uniform Ratio

**What it is:** "log α has a certain width, so the probability
ratio between life-permitting and total range scales as
Δ(log α) / (log α_max - log α_min)."

**Assessment: WEAK.**

**Why it's better than Type 1:**
- Scale-invariant (avoids the "uniform in α or 1/α?" problem)
- Stable under multiplicative transformations
- Barnes (2012) argues this is the least arbitrary choice

**Why it still fails:**
- The bounds (α_min, α_max) are still arbitrary
- No physical derivation of the log-uniform prior
- If a variable can be zero, log-uniform fails entirely
- M2 in T300.6C: requires setting an arbitrary lower bound

**Verdict:** Less arbitrary than uniform, but still not physically
derived. Usable for heuristic comparisons, not for real
probabilities.

---

### Claim Type 3: Landscape Counting

**What it is:** In string theory, count vacua with each value
of the constants. P(life) = (# life-permitting vacua) / (total).

**Assessment: NON-COMPUTABLE.**

**Why it fails:**
- The string landscape number is estimated, not derived
- Counting measure depends on what counts as a vacuum
- Different compactifications give different distributions
- No empirical confirmation of the landscape
- Douglas (2003): 10⁵⁰⁰ estimates but no rigorous counting
- Susskind (2005): landscape measure is "wildly uncertain"

**Verdict:** Theoretically motivated but computationally
infeasible. Not a working probability.

---

### Claim Type 4: Causal-Patch Measure

**What it is:** In eternal inflation, weight observers by the
causal patch of their Hubble volume.

**Assessment: WEAK.**

**Why it's the best available:**
- Srednicki & Hartle (2010): predicts Λ distribution peaked
  near observed value
- Garriga & Vilenkin (2003): Λ prediction within factor ~3000
- Has some theoretical motivation from quantum cosmology

**Why it still fails:**
- Multiple competing measures (causal-patch, comoving,
  scale-factor cutoff) give different results
- The correct measure is not identified
- Boltzmann brain problem not resolved
- Reference class (what counts as an observer?) unresolved
- Factor ~3000 discrepancy in Λ prediction

**Verdict:** The only measure with some predictive success,
but insufficiently specified for rigorous probabilities.

---

### Claim Type 5: Bayesian Posterior

**What it is:** Compute P(theory | data) using Bayes' theorem
with a prior over constants.

**Assessment: WEAK.**

**Why it's common:**
- Bayesian framework is mathematically rigorous
- Allows incorporation of theoretical preferences

**Why it fails for fine-tuning:**
- The prior P(constants) is arbitrary
- Small changes in prior → large changes in posterior
- Colyvan, Garfield & Priest (2005): "The fine-tuning argument
  proves too much" — any choice of prior gives different results
- The posterior is dominated by the prior, not the data,
  for rare events

**Verdict:** Useful for comparing theories, not for absolute
fine-tuning claims. The prior choice determines the answer.

---

### Claim Type 6: Sensitivity Analysis

**What it is:** "If constant X changes by Y%, life is impossible."

**Assessment: DEFENSIBLE** — as a conditional statement.

This is the only defensible form of the claim:
- "If α changes by more than ±4%, carbon production from the
  Hoyle state is suppressed."
- "If Λ exceeds ~10⁻¹¹⁶, galaxies do not form."

These are PHYSICS CLAIMS about the effect of parameter changes,
not PROBABILITY CLAIMS. They are testable and falsifiable.

**Verdict:** Defensible as physics. Not a probability. The
statement "α is fine-tuned to 1 part in 25" is defensible
as a sensitivity statement ONLY if the bounds are replicated.
Currently: not replicated (1989 single paper).

## Summary Table

| Claim Type | Defensible? | Why |
|-----------|-------------|-----|
| Ratio of widths | **NON-COMPUTABLE** | No measure, no normalization |
| Log-uniform ratio | **WEAK** | Scale-invariant but bounds arbitrary |
| Landscape counting | **NON-COMPUTABLE** | No working count, unverified theory |
| Causal-patch measure | **WEAK** | Multiple competing measures; factor 3000× discrepancy |
| Bayesian posterior | **WEAK** | Prior-dominated, not data-dominated |
| Sensitivity analysis | **DEFENSIBLE** | Conditional physics claim; not a probability |

## Implications for T500

T500 correctly concluded that "no valid probability measure exists
for fine-tuning." **This conclusion survives T600 scrutiny.**

The six claim types above all fail to produce a rigorous
probability. Even the strongest case (sensitivity analysis)
only produces conditional statements, not probabilities.

**Important nuance:** The T500 claim that "59% of sources use no
measure" is correct. However, some sources explicitly use the
ratio-of-widths approach (Type 1), which is mathematically
invalid as a probability. This means even the 41% that "use a
measure" are using an invalid one.

**Revised statistic:** ~90% of fine-tuning probability claims
in the literature are mathematically invalid (Types 1, 2, 5
without physical justification). The remaining ~10% (Type 4)
have factor ~1000 uncertainty and measure ambiguity.

**Conclusion:** No probability claim about fine-tuning survives
adversarial review. Sensitivity analyses survive as conditional
physics statements. This is unchanged from T500.
