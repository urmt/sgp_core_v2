# T300.6C — Adversarial Measure Attack

## Mission

Attack every probability measure. Show that all probability estimates
are artifacts of prior choice. Determine where conclusions flip,
disappear, or remain stable.

---

## Attack 1: All measures produce different numbers — none are correct

### The measure zoo

| Measure | Probability for α (T1) | Probability for α_G (T2) | Probability for Λ (T2) |
|---------|----------------------|-------------------------|----------------------|
| Agnostic (M0) | NaN | NaN | NaN |
| Linear (M1) | ~10⁻⁴ | ~10⁻³⁸ | ~10⁻¹¹⁸ |
| Log-uniform (M2) | ~0.5 | ~0.09 | ~0.03 |
| Jeffreys (M3) | ≈ M2 | ≈ M2 | ≈ M2 |
| Observer-weighted (M5) | Unknown | Unknown | Unknown |

**Attack:** The probability of the same physical event (α being in its
life-permitting range) varies by 10⁴⁰× between M1 and M2 for constants
like αG and Λ. This is not "uncertainty" — it is *complete
indeterminacy*. The result depends entirely on which measure you pick.

The engine correctly flags M1 as pathological for αG, v/M_Pl, and Λ.
But even the "corrected" M2 probability ~0.09 is not the "right"
answer — it's just the answer that doesn't have the obvious
pathology.

**Severity:** CRITICAL — all absolute probabilities are measure artifacts.

---

### Attack 1A: M2 (log-uniform) has an arbitrary lower bound

The log-uniform measure requires choosing a total range [log A, log B].
The upper bound is conventionally 0 (Planck scale = 1). The lower bound
is... where?

| Constant | Log range | Lower bound origin | Arbitrariness |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|---------------|
| α | [-10, 0] | ~10⁻¹⁰ (no physical lower bound) | ARBITRARY — α could be 0 |
| αs | [-10, 0] | ~10⁻¹⁰ (no physical lower bound) | ARBITRARY |
| μ | [-4, -0.38] | ~0.0001 (from m_p > m_e) | Physical — m_p > m_e is necessary |
| αG | [-45, 0] | ~10⁻⁴⁵ (from Carr & Rees T2) | **CIRCULAR** — uses the bound to set the prior |
| v/M_Pl | [-20, 0] | ~10⁻²⁰ (arbitrary) | ARBITRARY |
| Λ | [-124, 0] | ~10⁻¹²⁴ (arbitrary) | ARBITRARY — no known lower bound |

**Attack:** For αG, the prior lower bound is set to ~10⁻⁴⁵, which is
*derived from the Carr & Rees lower bound*. This means the prior range
is defined by the very bound it is supposed to measure — a circular
calculation. If the prior lower bound were 10⁻⁶⁰, the probability would
drop from 0.09 to 4/60 ≈ 0.07 — still ~same order, but the principle is
corrupt.

**Severity:** HIGH — the αG log-uniform probability is contaminated by
circular reasoning for the lower bound.

### Attack 1B: M2 fails for constants that can be zero

| Constant | Can it be zero? | Problem |
|----------|----------------|---------|
| α | Yes | log(0) = -∞ — undefined prior |
| αs | Yes | Same problem |
| μ | No (m_p > m_e) | Fine — μ > 0 physically |
| αG | Yes | log(0) undefined |
| v/M_Pl | Yes | log(0) undefined |
| Λ | Yes (and negative) | **DOUBLE PROBLEM** — Λ can be negative, which has no log at all |

**Attack:** The log-uniform measure is undefined for 3 of 6 constants
because they can be zero. For Λ, the measure is undefined because Λ can
be negative (AdS vacua). The engine works around this with a finite
lower bound, but the choice of lower bound is arbitrary for every
constant except μ.

**Severity:** CRITICAL — the measure does not apply to half the constants.

---

### Attack 1C: M1 (linear) gives tiny numbers — but is it wrong or is it honesty?

The linear measure gives P(αG) ~ 10⁻³⁸ and P(Λ) ~ 10⁻¹¹⁸. The engine
flags these as "pathological." But consider:

> If 99.99% of the literature agrees that αG is ~10⁻³⁹ relative to the
> Planck scale, and the question is "how likely is αG to be this small
> by chance," then P ~ 10⁻³⁹ might be the correct answer.

**Attack:** The pathology of M1 is not that it gives small numbers —
it's that the numbers are prior-range-dependent. But if the Planck scale
is the only natural reference point (as it is in quantum gravity), then
[0, 1] IS the natural prior range for dimensionless constants. The
"pathology" is a value judgment about what constitutes a fair prior.

**Rebuttal:** Log-uniform gives log-probabilities that depend on the
ratio of decades, not absolute values. For constants spanning 40+
decades, the log-uniform says "the ratio of constraining decades to
total decades" — which is a *different question* from "how likely is
this constant to have this value."

**Severity:** MODERATE — the log-linear pathology is real but the
interpretation is contested. The "correct" answer depends on whether
you think Planck-scale naturalness is the right reference.

## Attack 2: The observer-weighted measure would change everything — but is unknowable

### The claim

The engine excludes M5 (observer-weighted) because N_obs(x) is not
computable. This is true — but the *direction of the correction* is
unknown.

### Attack: M5 could strengthen OR weaken any conclusion

| Scenario | Effect on P(constant) | Meaning |
|----------|----------------------|---------|
| N_obs(x) peaks inside life-permitting range | Increases | Observed value is "typical" — no fine-tuning |
| N_obs(x) is flat | No change | M2 result stands |
| N_obs(x) peaks outside life-permitting range | Decreases | Universe looks fine-tuned because observer density is low at this parameter value — but observers can't exist where the density is zero |

**Attack:** The most plausible shape for N_obs(x) is that it peaks
somewhere inside the life-permitting region (by definition — there are
no observers outside it). This would *reduce* the apparent fine-tuning
by weighting the typical observer toward the center of the distribution.
The engine's exclusion of M5 is conservative for fine-tuning claims — it
produces stronger fine-tuning signals than an observer-weighted measure
might.

**Severity:** MODERATE — the exclusion of M5 means the engine's
probabilities are upper bounds on fine-tuning (the actual observer-
corrected values could be higher or lower).

## Attack 3: The Jeffreys prior is formally correct but impossible to apply

### The ideal

Jeffreys prior: P(θ) ∝ √det I(θ). For a multi-parameter system,
the Fisher information matrix I(θ) accounts for all parameter
covariances.

### The problem

Computing I(θ) for anthropic reasoning requires:
1. A likelihood function L(x | θ) = probability of observing data x
   given constants θ
2. This likelihood requires knowing how universes with constants θ
   produce observers
3. This is the same problem as computing N_obs(x) — we can't do it

**Attack:** Jeffreys prior is theoretically preferred but practically
unavailable. The log-uniform approximation (M2) is correct for single
scale parameters but fails for correlated systems.

**Severity:** MODERATE — known limitation, adequately documented.

## Attack 4: The normalization problem (McGrew, McGrew & Vestrup 2001)

### The argument

If the theoretical range of a constant is infinite (or semi-infinite),
any finite life-permitting range has probability ZERO under a uniform
prior, because:

P = (finite interval length) / (infinite total length) = 0

### Application to the corpus

| Constant | Theoretical range | Life-permitting range | Problem? |
|----------|------------------|----------------------|----------|
| α | [0, ∞) | [0.001, 0.1] | Yes — P = 0 under uniform prior |
| αs | [0, ∞) | [~0.07, ~0.2] | Yes — P = 0 |
| αG | [0, ∞) | [5.9×10⁻⁴⁵, 5.9×10⁻³⁶] | Yes — P = 0 |
| v/M_Pl | [0, ∞) | [10⁻¹⁸, 5×10⁻¹⁷] | Yes — P = 0 |
| Λ | (-∞, ∞) | [0, 10⁻¹¹⁶] | Yes — P = 0 |

**Attack:** Under any measure that is non-integrable over the full
theoretical range, the probability is strictly zero. The fine-tuning
argument as usually formulated (P = life-permitting / total range)
is mathematically invalid. This is the McGrew, McGrew & Vestrup
(2001) objection.

**Response in corpus:** Barnes (2012) and Collins (2009) argue that
the comparison range can be restricted to physically relevant regions
(e.g., the range over which we have theoretical control). But this
restriction is itself arbitrary.

**Severity:** CRITICAL — this is a mathematical objection, not a
physics objection. It applies to ALL probability-based fine-tuning
claims equally.

### Does the engine survive?

The engine restricts ranges to finite prior ranges (e.g., [-124, 0]
for log Λ). This is a defensible response — the comparison range
is finite. But the choice of range is not uniquely determined by
physics.

## Attack 5: The "no measure" row

### Attack: 59% of sources use no measure at all

From T300.4: 32 of 54 sources (59%) use no probability framework.
They provide bounds only. The engine extracts bounds from these
sources and then assigns probabilities to them.

**Attack:** The sources themselves did not claim their bounds
supported any particular probability. The engine imposes a
measure on bounds that were computed without one. This is
epistemically questionable: the bound is a physical fact ("α
changes by ±4% change C/O ratio"), but the probability is an
interpretation imposed by the analyst.

**Severity:** HIGH — the engine's probabilities are not contained in
the source literature. They are artifacts of the analysis framework.

## Attack 6: The pathological label is a value judgment

### The engine flags:

- αG + linear → PATHOLOGICAL
- v/M_Pl + linear → PATHOLOGICAL
- Λ + linear → PATHOLOGICAL

### Attack: What about:

- α + linear → OK (α ~ 10⁻², O(1) range)
- αs + linear → OK (αs ~ 0.1, O(1) range)
- μ + linear → OK (μ ~ 0.0005, moderate range)

But μ spans 10⁻⁴ of the [0, 1] range — should it also be pathological?

| Constant | Value | Linear P over [0,1] | Log P over 40 decades |
|----------|-------|---------------------|----------------------|
| μ | 0.0005 | 5×10⁻⁴ | ~0.3 |
| vev/M_Pl | 2×10⁻¹⁷ | 2×10⁻¹⁷ | ~0.4 |

**Attack:** The pathology threshold is continuous, not binary. μ
shows the same linear pathology as αG, just less extreme. The
engine's binary classification (PATHOLOGICAL vs. OK) is a
simplification that draws an arbitrary line between "pathological"
and "not pathological."

**Severity:** MODERATE — the binary classification is useful but
should not be interpreted as a physical distinction.

## Attack 7: Summary — What survives?

| Measure | Status | What can it say? |
|---------|--------|------------------|
| M0 (agnostic) | Honest | "The bounds exist. No probability assigned." |
| M1 (linear) | Pathological for 3/6 constants | Produces numbers that are artifacts of the Planck-scale prior upper bound |
| M2 (log-uniform) | Least-bad for positive-definite | Produces order-of-magnitude estimates; ratio-based, avoids pathological scaling |
| M3 (Jeffreys) | Unavailable for multi-parameter | Would be preferred but requires Fisher information matrix |
| M5 (observer) | Unknowable in direction | Could strengthen or weaken any conclusion by unknown factor |

### Final verdict on absolute probabilities

**No absolute probability in this analysis is defensible.** Every
number the engine produces depends on arbitrary prior range choices
that have no unique physical justification. The only defensible
outputs are:
1. **The bounds themselves** (α can change by X before C/O ratio degrades)
2. **Relative comparisons** (αs is ~100× more constrained than αG)
3. **Order-of-magnitude estimates** (log-uniform gives ~0.09 for αG)

Absolute probability values to 3+ significant figures are false
precision.
