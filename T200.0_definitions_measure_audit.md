# T200.0 — Definitions & Measure Audit

**Program:** T200 — Independent Fine-Tuning Analysis
**Phase:** 0 (foundational — must be settled before any data collection)
**Purpose:** Define every term and assumption that later phases will operationalize, so that all probability calculations are auditable from definition to result.

---

## 1. What Counts as a "Life-Permitting Universe"?

This is not a single binary. Multiple definitions exist in the literature, and the range of permissible constants changes dramatically depending on which is chosen. We will track **four increasingly restrictive tiers**:

### Tier 1 — Any Stable Chemistry
> The universe must permit at least one stable nucleus besides the proton, with sufficiently long-lived bound states to form atoms.

- Minimum requirement: at least one stable diproton or dineutron binding state, OR a stable nucleon–nucleon bound state (deuteron-like)
- Implicitly requires: at least one generation of quarks/leptons, at least one long-lived baryon, at least one stable charged lepton
- **If this fails:** no atoms, no molecules, no chemistry at all
- **Key constants:** α, αs, me/mp ratio, weak force strength
- **Literature anchor:** Harnik, Kribs, Perez (2006) "A Universe Without Weak Interactions"; Carr & Rees (1979)

### Tier 2 — Long-Lived Stars
> The universe must form stars that live long enough (~1 Gyr) to produce heavy elements via nucleosynthesis.

- Minimum main-sequence lifetime for at least some stars
- Requires: stable fusion pathways (pp-chain or CNO), radiative energy transport
- **Key constants:** G, α, mp, me, weak interaction rate
- **Literature anchor:** Adams (2019) "The Range of the Cosmological Constant"; Barnes (2012) "The Fine-Tuning of the Universe for Intelligent Life"

### Tier 3 — Complex Chemistry
> The universe must produce elements beyond H/He (carbon, oxygen, nitrogen, etc.) in sufficient abundance to form complex molecules.

- Requires: triple-alpha process with a resonant state (the Hoyle state), supernova nucleosynthesis, stable planet formation
- **Key constants:** α, αs, mp/me ratio, G, weak force, strong force binding energies
- **Literature anchor:** Barrow & Tipler (1986) §3–5; Carr & Rees (1979); Hogan (2000)
- **Note:** The Hoyle state in ¹²C is extremely sensitive to α and the strong force; shifting α by ~4% eliminates the resonance

### Tier 4 — Complex Information Processing
> The universe must permit sustained information-processing systems (e.g., DNA-based life, neural networks, or equivalent).

- Requires: long-term stable planetary environments, liquid medium (water? methane? alternatives?), energy gradients, molecular self-assembly
- **Key constants:** All of Tiers 1–3 plus fine details of molecular binding, solvent properties, and timescale matching
- **Literature anchor:** Lewis & Barnes (2016) "A Fortunate Universe"; Rees (2000) "Just Six Numbers"
- **Note:** This is the most controversial tier — the boundary is hard to define physically and merges into philosophical preference

### Operational Rule for T200

All later phases will report results **separately for each tier**, not aggregated. The document will explicitly state which tier is being used before any probability is reported. No single-number answer across tiers will be produced.

## 2. What Is the Sample Space?

### 2.1 Which Constants Vary?

**Included in every analysis:**
- α (fine-structure constant) — dimensionless
- αs (strong coupling at Z pole) — dimensionless
- G (gravitational constant) → expressed as dimensionless α_G = G m_p² / (ħc)
- me/mp (electron-to-proton mass ratio) — dimensionless
- Λ (cosmological constant in Planck units) — dimensionless
- m_weak / m_Pl (weak scale / Planck scale) — dimensionless

**Excluded:**
- Speed of light c — fixed by definition (1983 SI redefinition)
- Planck constant ħ — fixed by definition (2019 SI redefinition)
- Dimensionful constants expressed in human units — NO, convert to dimensionless
- Particle masses beyond mp, me — captured by mass ratios

**Rationale:** Only dimensionless constants can enter a physically meaningful fine-tuning analysis. Dimensionful constants are artifacts of our unit system. The six chosen constants span the Standard Model parameters plus gravity and cosmology.

### 2.2 One-at-a-Time vs. Simultaneous Variation

**Primary analysis:** Vary one constant while holding all others fixed at observed values.

**Secondary analysis (Phase 2-informed):** Vary correlated pairs jointly.

**No simultaneous variation of all 6+ constants** — the phase space is too large, interdependencies are too poorly known, and the result would be dominated by assumptions about measure rather than physics.

**Reported explicitly** in every probability statement: which constants varied, which were held fixed.

### 2.3 Variation Range

Each constant's range will be:

- **Upper bound:** The highest value at which Tier N is still possible
- **Lower bound:** The lowest value at which Tier N is still possible
- **Sources:** Published literature, with full citations
- **Uncertainty:** Each bound will carry an uncertainty classification:
  - **Firm:** Incontrovertible physics (e.g., a resonance disappearing)
  - **Moderate:** Model-dependent but robust across reasonable assumptions
  - **Weak:** Speculative or dependent on unverified assumptions

Where no published bound exists, we note "UNKNOWN — further research needed" rather than fabricating a bound.

## 3. What Probability Measure Is Being Assumed?

This is the single most consequential choice in any fine-tuning analysis. Different measures can change results by hundreds of orders of magnitude. We will evaluate **four distinct measures** and never collapse them.

### Measure A — Uniform in Linear Value

> The prior probability of a constant is uniform across its theoretically allowed range.

- **Formula:** P(x) = 1 / (x_max − x_min) within [x_min, x_max]
- **Strengths:** Simple, transparent, most common in fine-tuning literature
- **Weaknesses:**
  - Choice of range boundaries dominates the result
  - "Theoretically allowed range" is not well-defined for most constants
  - Physically unnatural for quantities that span many orders of magnitude
- **Used by:** Some anthropic analyses (e.g., Weinberg 1987 for Λ)

### Measure B — Uniform in Log Value

> The prior probability is uniform in log(x), equivalent to assuming no preferred scale.

- **Formula:** P(log x) = 1 / (log x_max − log x_min) for x > 0
- **Strengths:** Scale-invariant, natural for quantities like Λ that span ~120 orders of magnitude
- **Weaknesses:**
  - Requires x > 0 (most constants are positive, but signs matter)
  - Still sensitive to range boundaries (but less dramatically)
  - Implicitly assumes the constant can vary continuously over many decades
- **Used by:** Tegmark (1998), many modern analyses

### Measure C — Observer-Weighted (Anthropic)

> The probability is proportional to the number of observers that would exist in a universe with those constants.

- **Formula:** P(x | observers exist) ∝ P(x) × N_obs(x), where N_obs(x) is the expected number of observers
- **Strengths:** Formally correct under the self-sampling assumption
- **Weaknesses:**
  - N_obs(x) is extremely difficult to compute (what counts as an observer?)
  - Handles Boltzmann brain problems poorly
  - Can produce absurd results when N_obs diverges
  - Leads to measure paradoxes in infinite cosmologies
- **Used by:** Bostrom (2002) "Anthropic Bias"; Vilenkin (2007) "Many Worlds in One"

### Measure D — No Preferred Measure (Agnostic)

> We do not assert any probability measure. Instead, we report the life-permitting range as a simple interval and refuse to convert it to a probability.

- **Output format:** "[Constant] must lie in [range] for [Tier] to be possible. No probability assigned."
- **Strengths:** Fully honest about what physics can tell us
- **Weaknesses:** Does not answer the question "how improbable?" — some find this unsatisfying
- **Used by:** Some physicists who reject the fine-tuning inference altogether (e.g., Stenger 2011 "The Fallacy of Fine-Tuning")

## 4. Operational Rules for All T200 Phases

### R1 — Separate Facts from Interpretations
Every document will have two clearly labeled sections:
- **Empirical findings** (measured values, life-permitting bounds, citations)
- **Model-dependent results** (probabilities, which depend on Measure A–D)

No statement in the Empirical section will use probability language.

### R2 — Report All Measures, Never Just One
Every probability claim will be reported for at least Measures A, B, and D. Measure C will be labeled as "more speculative" whenever used.

### R3 — No Multiplication Without Justification
No probability product will be computed across multiple constants unless Phase 2 (Dependency Structure) has explicitly classified those constants as independent. Any product will include the explicit justification and the dependency classification.

### R4 — Uncertainties on Everything
Every numerical bound will have an uncertainty classification (Firm / Moderate / Weak). Every probability will have a sensitivity range.

### R5 — No Single Headline Number
The final output will not be a single probability. It will be a structured document organized by Tier, by Measure, and by constant, showing how results vary with assumptions.

---

## 5. Immediate Consequences

### What is now forbidden:
- "The fine-structure constant is fine-tuned to 1 part in 10¹⁰⁰" — violates R1 (no probability), R2 (no measure specified), R4 (no uncertainty)
- "The probability of a life-permitting universe is 10⁻¹²³" — violates R5 (single headline number), R2 (no alternative measures reported)

### Required replacement:
> "Under Tier 2 (long-lived stars), varying α alone while holding other constants fixed, the life-permitting range is [range] based on [citation]. Under Measure A (uniform linear), this gives probability P_A = [range]. Under Measure B (uniform log), P_B = [range]. Under Measure D, no probability is assigned. Uncertainty in bounds is [classification]. Sensitivity to alternative assumptions is discussed in §__."
